# Active Listening — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creator

Active listening was pioneered by Carl Rogers and Richard Farson in their 1957 paper "Active Listening" (University of Chicago Industrial Relations Center). Rogers, the founder of person-centered therapy, demonstrated that **empathic listening alone** — without advice, diagnosis, or judgment — is often sufficient to help people solve their own problems.

Most people listen to reply. Active listeners listen to understand.

## The Rogers Triad

Carl Rogers identified three conditions that create a space for genuine communication:

1. **Congruence (Genuineness):** Be real. Don't perform empathy you don't feel.
2. **Unconditional Positive Regard:** Accept the person without judgment, even if you disagree with their actions.
3. **Empathic Understanding:** See the world from their perspective, not yours.

When all three are present, people naturally move toward clarity and growth — often without any advice at all.

## The 5 Levels of Listening

### Level 1: Ignoring
Not listening at all. Physically present but mentally absent. Checking your phone while someone talks.

### Level 2: Pretending
Going through the motions. Nodding, saying "uh-huh," but not processing. The speaker usually senses this.

### Level 3: Selective
Hearing only the parts that interest you or confirm your existing beliefs. Dangerous because you think you listened, but you missed the actual message.

### Level 4: Attentive
Fully focused on the words being said. You can repeat back accurately. Good, but still limited — you hear the content but may miss the meaning.

### Level 5: Empathic
Listening with the intent to understand the speaker's frame of reference. You hear the words, the feelings, the context, and the unspoken meaning. This is the level that transforms relationships.

**The gap between Level 4 and Level 5 is where most people get stuck.** Attentive listening focuses on YOUR understanding. Empathic listening focuses on THEIR experience.

## The 12 Roadblocks (Thomas Gordon)

1. **Ordering:** "You need to..." / "Stop doing that and..."
2. **Warning:** "If you don't... then..." / "You'd better..."
3. **Moralizing:** "You should..." / "It's your responsibility to..."
4. **Advising:** "What I would do is..." / "Have you tried...?"
5. **Lecturing:** "The fact is..." / "Studies show that..."
6. **Judging:** "You're wrong." / "That's a bad idea."
7. **Praising (evaluatively):** "You're so smart!" (shifts focus to your judgment)
8. **Name-calling:** "You're being paranoid." / "That's immature."
9. **Interpreting:** "The real reason you feel that way is..."
10. **Reassuring:** "Don't worry, it'll be fine." / "You're overreacting."
11. **Questioning (interrogating):** "When? Where? Why? Who? How?"
12. **Distracting:** "Let's talk about something more positive."

## The Big 4 Listening Killers

| Killer | What It Sounds Like | Why It Fails |
|--------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Judging** | "Well, that was a mistake." | Speaker shuts down; no longer safe to share |
| **Advising** | "You should just..." | Implies speaker can't solve their own problems |
| **Diagnosing** | "Your real problem is..." | Speaker feels analyzed, not heard |
| **One-Upping** | "That's nothing -- let me tell you about MY..." | Speaker's experience is dismissed |

**The most insidious barrier: Advising.** It FEELS helpful. But unsolicited advice communicates: "I know better than you, and you're not capable of figuring this out."

## Three Types of Silence (침묵의 3가지 종류)

| Type | Signs | Your Response |
|------|-------|--------------|
| **Thinking Silence** (생각하는 침묵) | Eyes moving, slight head tilt | Wait. They're processing. Don't interrupt. |
| **Emotional Silence** (감정적 침묵) | Tears, looking down, shallow breathing | Be present. Gentle "take your time." No words needed. |
| **Resistant Silence** (저항의 침묵) | Arms crossed, looking away, jaw tight | Change approach. "I sense this isn't the right time. What would help?" |

## When Active Listening Is NOT Enough

- **Emergency situations:** Act first, listen later.
- **Boundary violations:** "I hear that you're upset, AND I won't accept being spoken to this way."
- **Information exchange:** Just answer factual questions directly.
- **You're emotionally depleted:** Say so: "I want to give this the attention it deserves. Can we talk in an hour?"
