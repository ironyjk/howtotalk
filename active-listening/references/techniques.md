# Active Listening Techniques — Quick Reference

## The HEAR Method (Simplified Framework)

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **H** — Halt | Stop what you're doing. Full attention. | Put phone away, close laptop, turn body toward speaker |
| **E** — Empathize | Try to feel what they feel. | "If I were in their position, I'd probably feel..." |
| **A** — Anticipate (resist it) | Notice your urge to respond, but don't act on it yet. | Catch yourself preparing a rebuttal and let it go |
| **R** — Respond | Only after fully hearing, respond with a listening technique. | Paraphrase, reflect, or ask a clarifying question |

## Technique Cheat Sheet

### Paraphrasing Templates

| Situation | Template |
|-----------|----------|
| Factual content | "So the main issue is [X], and it happened because [Y]." |
| Complex story | "Let me see if I've got this — first [A] happened, then [B], which led to [C]." |
| Vague statement | "When you say [their word], do you mean [your interpretation]?" |
| Multiple issues | "It sounds like there are two things going on: [X] and [Y]. Which feels more pressing?" |

### Feeling Reflection Templates

| Speaker's Energy | Possible Feelings | Template |
|-----------------|-------------------|----------|
| Frustrated, tense | Angry, overwhelmed, stuck | "That sounds really frustrating." |
| Quiet, withdrawn | Sad, hurt, disappointed | "It seems like that hit you hard." |
| Rapid speech, scattered | Anxious, worried, overwhelmed | "There's a lot on your mind right now." |
| Flat, monotone | Exhausted, resigned, hopeless | "It sounds like you've been carrying this for a while." |
| Excited but nervous | Hopeful, scared, vulnerable | "There's excitement there, but also some fear?" |

### Feeling Vocabulary

**Low intensity → High intensity:**

| Category | Low | Medium | High |
|----------|-----|--------|------|
| Anger | Annoyed, bothered | Frustrated, resentful | Furious, enraged |
| Sadness | Disappointed, down | Hurt, discouraged | Devastated, heartbroken |
| Fear | Uneasy, nervous | Worried, anxious | Terrified, panicked |
| Joy | Pleased, satisfied | Happy, excited | Elated, ecstatic |
| Confusion | Puzzled, curious | Uncertain, conflicted | Lost, overwhelmed |
| Shame | Embarrassed, awkward | Guilty, inadequate | Worthless, humiliated |

**Tip:** Match the intensity. If someone is "devastated," reflecting "you seem a bit upset" minimizes their experience. If they're "a little annoyed," reflecting "you're furious" escalates it.

## Clarifying Questions — Good vs Bad

### Good (Open, Curious)
- "Can you say more about that?"
- "What was that like for you?"
- "What matters most to you in this situation?"
- "What would the ideal outcome look like?"
- "How long has this been going on?"
- "What have you already tried?"

### Bad (Leading, Judging, Advising-in-Disguise)
- "Don't you think you should...?" (advising)
- "Why would you do that?" (judging)
- "Have you considered that maybe you're overreacting?" (diagnosing)
- "Wouldn't it be better to just...?" (advising)
- "Are you sure that's what happened?" (doubting)
- "Why didn't you just...?" (judging past behavior)

## The Validation Sequence

When someone is emotionally activated, use this sequence before anything else:

1. **Acknowledge:** "I can see this is important to you."
2. **Validate:** "It makes sense that you'd feel that way, given [their context]."
3. **Normalize:** "A lot of people would feel the same in your position."
4. **Invite:** "Tell me more about what's going on."

**Do NOT skip to problem-solving.** The brain cannot process solutions while the amygdala is firing. Validation calms the amygdala. Then logic can enter.

## Micro-Skills for Daily Practice

### The 10% Rule
In your next 10 conversations, try to talk 10% less than you normally would. Just that small shift changes the dynamic.

### The Curiosity Reframe
When you feel the urge to give advice, ask yourself: "What am I curious about?" Then ask THAT question instead.

### The 3-Beat Pause
After someone finishes speaking: Beat 1 (process what they said), Beat 2 (notice your emotional response), Beat 3 (choose your response consciously).

### The Mirror Test
After a conversation, ask yourself: "Could the other person tell that I understood them?" If you're not sure, you probably didn't show it enough.

## Common Mistakes Even Good Listeners Make

1. **Reflecting too much** — It starts to feel like a therapy session. Mix techniques naturally.
2. **Asking too many questions** — The speaker starts feeling interrogated. Let statements breathe.
3. **Reflecting the wrong emotion** — If you guess wrong, that's OK. But if you keep getting it wrong, you're projecting your own feelings.
4. **Performing empathy** — People can sense when you're "doing a technique" vs genuinely caring. Congruence matters more than skill.
5. **Listening as a strategy** — If you're listening to manipulate or extract information, it's not active listening. Intent matters.
