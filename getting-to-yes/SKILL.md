# Getting to Yes — Principled Negotiation Skill

> "The reason you negotiate is to produce something better than the results you can obtain without negotiating." — Roger Fisher

## Overview

Getting to Yes (예스를 이끌어내는 협상법) presents principled negotiation (원칙적 협상), developed at the Harvard Negotiation Project. It rejects the choice between "soft" (relationship-focused, yielding) and "hard" (position-focused, aggressive) negotiation, offering a third way: be SOFT on people and HARD on the problem.

## The Three Negotiation Styles

| Dimension | Soft (유약한) | Hard (강경한) | Principled (원칙적) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Participants are... | Friends | Adversaries | Problem-solvers |
| Goal | Agreement | Victory | Wise outcome efficiently |
| People vs Problem | Soft on both | Hard on both | Soft on people, hard on problem |
| Trust | Trust others | Distrust others | Proceed independent of trust |
| Positions | Change easily | Dig in | Focus on interests, not positions |
| Bottom line | Disclose | Mislead | Develop BATNA |
| Offers | Make offers | Make threats | Explore interests |
| Yield to... | Pressure | Nothing | Principle/criteria |

## The 4 Principles

### Principle 1: Separate the People from the Problem (사람과 문제를 분리하라)

Every negotiation has two dimensions: the substance (내용) and the relationship (관계). Most negotiators entangle them.

**Three people problems:**

**1. Perception (인식)**
- Each side sees what it wants to see
- Don't deduce their intentions from your fears
- Don't blame them for your problem
- Discuss each other's perceptions explicitly
- Give them a stake in the outcome by involving them in the process
- Make proposals consistent with their values (face-saving)

**2. Emotion (감정)**
- Recognize and understand emotions — yours and theirs
- Make emotions explicit: "I'm feeling frustrated. Are you?"
- Allow the other side to let off steam — don't react to emotional outbursts
- Use symbolic gestures (apology, acknowledgment, shared meal)

**3. Communication (소통)**
- Listen actively and acknowledge what is being said
- Speak to be understood, not to impress
- Speak about yourself, not about them ("I feel let down" not "You broke your word")
- Speak for a purpose — not everything needs to be said

**Key technique — Build a working relationship:**
Before the negotiation starts, establish rapport. Once emotions rise, it's too late to build trust.

### Principle 2: Focus on Interests, Not Positions (입장이 아닌 이해관계에 집중하라)

**Position (입장):** What someone SAYS they want — "I want $500K"
**Interest (이해관계):** WHY they want it — security, fairness, recognition, autonomy

**The classic example:**
Two sisters fight over an orange (positions). One wants the peel for baking, the other wants the juice. If they'd discussed interests, both could have 100%.

**How to identify interests:**
1. Ask "WHY?" — "Why do you want that?"
2. Ask "WHY NOT?" — "What would be wrong with this alternative?"
3. Realize each side has MULTIPLE interests
4. The most powerful interests are basic human needs:
   - Security (안전)
   - Economic well-being (경제적 안녕)
   - Sense of belonging (소속감)
   - Recognition (인정)
   - Control over one's life (자율성)

**How to discuss interests:**
- Make your interests vivid and specific — not "fairness" but "if I accept this, I'll lose $X per month"
- Acknowledge their interests as legitimate: "I understand that timeline matters to you"
- Be hard on the problem, soft on the people
- Look forward, not backward — "What should we do?" not "Who's to blame?"

### Principle 3: Invent Options for Mutual Gain (상호 이익을 위한 선택지를 만들어라)

Most negotiations fail because people see the pie as fixed. Expand the pie first.

**Four obstacles to creative options:**
1. **Premature judgment** — Evaluating ideas kills generation
2. **Searching for the single answer** — There's always more than one solution
3. **Assumption of a fixed pie** — "More for you = less for me"
4. **Thinking that solving their problem is their problem** — You should help solve BOTH sides' problems

**Techniques to generate options:**

**Brainstorming session rules (브레인스토밍 규칙):**
1. Separate inventing from deciding — no criticism during generation
2. Broaden the options — don't look for a single answer
3. Search for mutual gains — identify shared interests
4. Make their decision easy — what would they say yes to?

**The Circle Chart (원형 차트):**
```
Step 1: PROBLEM — What's wrong? (symptoms)
Step 2: ANALYSIS — What are the causes? (diagnosis)
Step 3: APPROACHES — What strategies could address the causes? (theoretical solutions)
Step 4: ACTION IDEAS — What specific steps could be taken? (practical proposals)
```

**Dovetailing interests (이해관계 맞물리기):**
- Different interests can be complementary, not conflicting
- They value A more, you value B more → trade A for B
- Different risk tolerance → one takes more risk for more reward
- Different time preferences → short-term vs long-term tradeoff

### Principle 4: Insist on Objective Criteria (객관적 기준을 고집하라)

When interests conflict, use objective standards rather than a contest of will.

**Types of objective criteria:**
- Market value (시장 가치)
- Precedent (선례)
- Scientific judgment (과학적 판단)
- Professional standards (전문가 기준)
- Costs (원가)
- Moral standards (도덕적 기준)
- Tradition (관행)
- Reciprocity (상호성)
- Court ruling (법원 판결)
- Equal treatment (동등 대우)

**Types of fair procedures:**
- Taking turns (순번제)
- Drawing lots (제비뽑기)
- Letting someone else decide (제3자 결정)
- "One cuts, the other chooses" (한 사람이 자르고, 다른 사람이 선택)

**How to use objective criteria:**
1. Frame each issue as a joint search for objective criteria
2. Ask: "What's your basis for that?" / "How did you arrive at that figure?"
3. Reason and be open to reason
4. Never yield to pressure, only to principle

## BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement (합의 불발 시 최선의 대안)

The single most important concept in negotiation. Your BATNA is your power.

**Why BATNA matters:**
- Protects you from accepting a deal that's too unfavorable
- Protects you from rejecting a deal you should accept
- The better your BATNA, the more negotiating power you have

**Developing your BATNA:**
1. **List** all alternatives if negotiations fail
2. **Improve** the most promising alternatives — make them real
3. **Select** the best one — this is your BATNA
4. **Evaluate** the other side's BATNA — it tells you their power
5. **Never reveal** your BATNA unless it's very strong

**ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement (합의 가능 영역):**
```
Your BATNA ──────[====ZOPA====]────── Their BATNA
          Your          Their
          reservation    reservation
          point          point
```

If your reservation point (walk-away price) is WORSE than theirs, there's a ZOPA. If not, no deal should be made.

## Advanced Techniques

### The One-Text Procedure (단일 텍스트 절차)

When multiple parties can't agree, a mediator (or one side) creates a single draft:
1. Ask all sides about their interests (not positions)
2. Write a draft that addresses all interests as best as possible
3. Show it to each side: "What's wrong with this?" (not "Do you agree?")
4. Revise based on criticism
5. Repeat until no further improvements can be made
6. Present the final text: "This is the best I can do. Accept or reject."

Used in the Camp David Accords (1978). 23 drafts. It worked.

### Negotiation Jujitsu (협상 유도술)

When the other side uses hard bargaining tactics:
1. **Don't push back** — redirect their energy
2. **Don't attack their position** — look behind it for interests
3. **Don't defend your ideas** — invite criticism: "What's wrong with this approach?"
4. **Ask questions** instead of making statements
5. **Use silence** — people are uncomfortable with it and will fill it

### Dealing with Dirty Tricks (더러운 수법 대처)

| Trick | Response |
|---|---|
| Good cop / Bad cop | Name it: "Is this a good-cop/bad-cop routine?" |
| Extreme opening demand | Ask for objective justification: "How did you arrive at that?" |
| "Take it or leave it" | Ignore it. Treat it as one option. Explore alternatives. |
| Deliberate deception | Verify independently. "Trust but verify." |
| Personal attacks | Name it and redirect: "Let's get back to the problem." |
| Escalating demands | Call it out. Take a break. |
| Lock-in tactics | Reframe: "Let's find a creative solution that doesn't bind either of us." |
| Pressure tactics | "I never make important decisions under time pressure." |
| Calculated delay | Set procedural deadlines. Make delay costly. |

## Anti-patterns

1. **Positional bargaining** — Anchoring to a number and haggling. Wastes time, damages relationships, produces arbitrary outcomes.
2. **No BATNA preparation** — Negotiating without knowing your alternatives is negotiating blind.
3. **Confusing interests with positions** — "I want the corner office" (position) vs. "I need to feel valued and have a quiet space to focus" (interests).
4. **Fixed pie assumption** — Assuming what benefits one side must hurt the other. Always look for trades.
5. **Yielding to pressure** — Accepting a deal because they pushed hard, not because it meets your interests.
6. **Revealing a weak BATNA** — "We really need this deal" destroys your power.
7. **Attacking people, not problems** — "You're being unreasonable" instead of "This proposal doesn't address my safety concern."
8. **Premature evaluation** — Shooting down options during brainstorming kills creativity.
9. **Single-issue focus** — Negotiating only price when scope, timeline, payment terms, and other variables could all be traded.
10. **Ignoring relationship** — Winning the deal but destroying the relationship. Long-term value comes from ongoing collaboration.
