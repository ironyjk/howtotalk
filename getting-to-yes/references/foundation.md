# Getting to Yes — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creators

*Getting to Yes* (1981, 3rd ed. 2011) was written by Roger Fisher and William Ury at the Harvard Negotiation Project, part of the Program on Negotiation at Harvard Law School. It introduced "principled negotiation" (원칙적 협상) as a third way beyond soft (relationship-focused, yielding) and hard (position-focused, aggressive) negotiation: be SOFT on people and HARD on the problem.

## The Three Negotiation Styles

| Dimension | Soft (유약한) | Hard (강경한) | Principled (원칙적) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Participants are... | Friends | Adversaries | Problem-solvers |
| Goal | Agreement | Victory | Wise outcome efficiently |
| People vs Problem | Soft on both | Hard on both | Soft on people, hard on problem |
| Trust | Trust others | Distrust others | Proceed independent of trust |
| Positions | Change easily | Dig in | Focus on interests, not positions |
| Bottom line | Disclose | Mislead | Develop BATNA |
| Offers | Make offers | Make threats | Explore interests |
| Yield to... | Pressure | Nothing | Principle/criteria |

## The 4 Principles — Deep Theory

### Principle 1: Separate the People from the Problem (사람과 문제를 분리하라)

Every negotiation has two dimensions: substance (내용) and relationship (관계). Most negotiators entangle them.

**Three people problems:**

**1. Perception (인식)**
- Each side sees what it wants to see
- Don't deduce their intentions from your fears
- Don't blame them for your problem
- Discuss each other's perceptions explicitly
- Give them a stake in the outcome by involving them in the process
- Make proposals consistent with their values (face-saving)

**2. Emotion (감정)**
- Recognize and understand emotions — yours and theirs
- Make emotions explicit: "I'm feeling frustrated. Are you?"
- Allow the other side to let off steam — don't react to emotional outbursts
- Use symbolic gestures (apology, acknowledgment, shared meal)

**3. Communication (소통)**
- Listen actively and acknowledge what is being said
- Speak to be understood, not to impress
- Speak about yourself, not about them ("I feel let down" not "You broke your word")

### Principle 2: Focus on Interests, Not Positions (입장이 아닌 이해관계에 집중하라)

**Position (입장):** What someone SAYS they want — "I want $500K"
**Interest (이해관계):** WHY they want it — security, fairness, recognition, autonomy

**The classic example:**
Two sisters fight over an orange (positions). One wants the peel for baking, the other wants the juice. If they'd discussed interests, both could have 100%.

**The most powerful interests are basic human needs:**
- Security (안전)
- Economic well-being (경제적 안녕)
- Sense of belonging (소속감)
- Recognition (인정)
- Control over one's life (자율성)

### Principle 3: Invent Options for Mutual Gain (상호 이익을 위한 선택지를 만들어라)

**Four obstacles to creative options:**
1. Premature judgment — Evaluating ideas kills generation
2. Searching for the single answer — There's always more than one solution
3. Assumption of a fixed pie — "More for you = less for me"
4. Thinking that solving their problem is their problem

**The Circle Chart (원형 차트):**
```
Step 1: PROBLEM — What's wrong? (symptoms)
Step 2: ANALYSIS — What are the causes? (diagnosis)
Step 3: APPROACHES — What strategies could address the causes?
Step 4: ACTION IDEAS — What specific steps could be taken?
```

**Dovetailing interests (이해관계 맞물리기):**
- Different interests can be complementary, not conflicting
- They value A more, you value B more -> trade A for B
- Different risk tolerance -> one takes more risk for more reward
- Different time preferences -> short-term vs long-term tradeoff

### Principle 4: Insist on Objective Criteria (객관적 기준을 고집하라)

**Types of objective criteria:**
Market value, precedent, scientific judgment, professional standards, costs, moral standards, tradition, reciprocity, court ruling, equal treatment.

**Types of fair procedures:**
Taking turns, drawing lots, letting someone else decide, "one cuts, the other chooses."

## BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement (합의 불발 시 최선의 대안)

The single most important concept in negotiation. Your BATNA is your power.

**ZOPA — Zone of Possible Agreement (합의 가능 영역):**
```
Your BATNA ------[====ZOPA====]------ Their BATNA
```

## Advanced Techniques

### The One-Text Procedure (단일 텍스트 절차)
When multiple parties can't agree, a mediator creates a single draft:
1. Ask all sides about their interests (not positions)
2. Write a draft that addresses all interests
3. Show it to each side: "What's wrong with this?"
4. Revise based on criticism; repeat
5. Present the final text: "Accept or reject."

Used in the Camp David Accords (1978). 23 drafts. It worked.

### Negotiation Jujitsu (협상 유도술)
When the other side uses hard bargaining:
1. Don't push back — redirect their energy
2. Don't attack their position — look behind it for interests
3. Don't defend your ideas — invite criticism
4. Ask questions instead of making statements
5. Use silence

### Dealing with Dirty Tricks (더러운 수법 대처)

| Trick | Response |
|---|---|
| Good cop / Bad cop | Name it: "Is this a good-cop/bad-cop routine?" |
| Extreme opening demand | Ask for objective justification |
| "Take it or leave it" | Ignore it. Explore alternatives. |
| Deliberate deception | Verify independently. "Trust but verify." |
| Personal attacks | Name it and redirect to the problem |
| Pressure tactics | "I never make important decisions under time pressure." |
