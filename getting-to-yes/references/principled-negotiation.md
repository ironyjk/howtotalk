# Principled Negotiation — Framework Reference

> From *Getting to Yes* by Roger Fisher & William Ury (Harvard Negotiation Project)

---

## The 4 Principles at a Glance

| # | Principle | Core Question | Key Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Separate People from Problem | Are we attacking each other or the problem? | Be soft on people, hard on the problem |
| 2 | Focus on Interests, Not Positions | Why do they want that? | Ask "Why?" and "Why not?" |
| 3 | Invent Options for Mutual Gain | Can we expand the pie? | Brainstorm before deciding |
| 4 | Insist on Objective Criteria | What's the fair standard? | Use market value, precedent, expert opinion |

## Interest Mapping Template

### How to Uncover Interests

**Questions to ask yourself:**
- Why do I want this?
- What need does this position serve?
- What would happen if I didn't get this?
- What am I really worried about?

**Questions to ask them:**
- "Help me understand why this is important to you."
- "What would this achieve for you?"
- "What concerns would you have with an alternative approach?"
- "If you could design the ideal outcome, what would it look like?"

## Options Generation — The Circle Chart

Use this structured process to move from problem to actionable proposals:

```
               IN THEORY                     IN THE REAL WORLD
            ┌─────────────┐               ┌─────────────────┐
            │             │               │                 │
Step 2:     │  ANALYSIS   │  Step 3:      │   APPROACHES    │
Diagnose    │  What are   │  Prescribe    │   What broad    │

            │             │               │                 │
            └──────▲──────┘               └───────┬─────────┘
                   │                              │
                   │                              ▼
            ┌──────┴──────┐               ┌───────┴─────────┐
            │             │               │                 │
Step 1:     │  PROBLEM    │  Step 4:      │   ACTION IDEAS  │
What's      │  What's     │  What         │   What specific │
wrong?      │  wrong?     │  might be     │   steps?        │
            │  Symptoms   │  done?        │                 │
            │             │               │                 │
            └─────────────┘               └─────────────────┘
```

**Example application:**

| Step | Content |
|---|---|
| **Problem:** | Late deliveries causing customer complaints |
| **Analysis:** | Supplier lead times increased; warehouse understaffed on Fridays |

## Objective Criteria Finder

### Types of Standards

**Market-based:**
- Comparable sales/transactions
- Market price/rate
- Industry benchmarks
- Auction results

**Precedent-based:**
- Previous agreements between same parties
- Standard industry contracts
- Legal precedents
- Historical pricing

**Expert/Scientific:**
- Appraisals
- Technical assessments
- Cost analysis
- Risk assessments

**Legal/Regulatory:**
- Laws and regulations
- Building codes
- Tax assessments
- Government standards

**Moral/Community:**
- Reciprocity ("Would you accept this if roles were reversed?")
- Equal treatment
- Custom/Tradition
- Religious/ethical standards

### How to Introduce Objective Criteria

1. Frame jointly: "Let's figure out the fair price together."
2. Ask what standards THEY would suggest: "What standard do you think is appropriate?"
3. Present your research: "Here's what I found about market rates..."
4. Be open to their standards if legitimate
5. Never yield to pressure, only to principle: "I can't justify this to my [board/partner/team] — what objective basis can we use?"

## Negotiation Preparation Checklist

### Before the Meeting

- [ ] Identified my interests (not just my position)
- [ ] Hypothesized their interests
- [ ] Developed and strengthened my BATNA
- [ ] Estimated their BATNA
- [ ] Determined my reservation point
- [ ] Generated 3+ options for mutual gain
- [ ] Researched objective criteria/standards
- [ ] Considered relationship dynamics (perception, emotion, communication)
- [ ] Prepared to separate people from the problem
- [ ] Planned opening — interests, not positions

### During the Meeting

- [ ] Listening more than talking
- [ ] Asking "Why?" and "Why not?" about their positions
- [ ] Brainstorming options BEFORE evaluating them
- [ ] Using objective criteria to resolve disagreements
- [ ] Stepping to their side (empathy) when tensions rise
- [ ] Avoiding positional anchoring
- [ ] Not revealing a weak BATNA

### After the Meeting

- [ ] Documented agreements (Who/What/When)
- [ ] Assessed: Did we address all interests?
- [ ] Checked: Is this better than my BATNA?
- [ ] Preserved the relationship
- [ ] Identified lessons for next time

## Common Situations and Responses

### "Take it or leave it" (최후통첩)
- Don't accept or reject immediately
- Ask: "Is this really your final offer, or is there room to discuss?"
- Explore WHY they've drawn this line
- Present your BATNA without threat: "I need to compare this with my alternatives"

### They won't reveal interests
- Share yours first to build reciprocity
- Hypothesize: "Could it be that you're concerned about ___?"
- Ask procedural questions: "What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable sharing?"

### Power imbalance (they're much stronger)
- Strengthen your BATNA — it's your only real source of power
- Form coalitions (allies, other parties with aligned interests)
- Use legitimacy: "The industry standard is..."
- Make the cost of no-agreement clear to them

### Multiple parties
- Use the One-Text Procedure (mediator drafts, all criticize, revise)
- Identify the parties with the most influence
- Build coalitions around shared interests
- Map all parties' interests in a matrix
