# SCARF Domain Quick Reference Guide

## Domain Interaction Matrix

Domains interact and amplify each other. Threatening one domain often cascades into others.

| Primary Threat | Often Cascades Into | Example |
|---------------|--------------------|---------| 
| Status | Fairness, Relatedness | "Why was SHE promoted instead of me?" (Status → Fairness → Relatedness isolation) |
| Certainty | Autonomy, Status | "I don't know what's expected, so I can't perform well" (Certainty → Status fear) |
| Autonomy | Status, Fairness | "I'm micromanaged but others aren't" (Autonomy → Fairness → Status) |
| Relatedness | Status, Certainty | "I'm excluded, so I don't know what's happening" (Relatedness → Certainty) |
| Fairness | Relatedness, Status | "The rules aren't applied equally, and everyone sees it" (Fairness → Status → Relatedness) |

## SCARF Profiles

Different people have different sensitivity profiles. Some care deeply about Status but are relaxed about Autonomy; others need Certainty above all.

### How to Identify Someone's Primary Domain

**High Status sensitivity:**
- Frequently mentions credentials, experience, past achievements
- Reacts strongly to public correction
- Motivated by titles, recognition, leaderboards
- Ask: "What matters most — being respected or being liked?"

**High Certainty sensitivity:**
- Asks many clarifying questions
- Uncomfortable with ambiguity or "let's figure it out as we go"
- Prefers detailed plans, written instructions
- Ask: "How do you feel about open-ended projects?"

**High Autonomy sensitivity:**
- Pushes back on rigid processes
- Prefers "what" over "how" instructions
- Works best with freedom, worst under surveillance
- Ask: "Do you prefer clear instructions or freedom to figure it out?"

**High Relatedness sensitivity:**
- Invests in personal relationships at work
- Uncomfortable in purely transactional interactions
- Needs to feel "part of the group" before performing
- Ask: "How important is it to you that your team likes you?"

**High Fairness sensitivity:**
- Notices inconsistencies in rule application
- Vocal about process and transparency
- May seem "difficult" but actually values justice
- Ask: "What bothers you more — losing or losing unfairly?"

## Conversation Starters by Domain

### To Boost Status
- "You're the expert on this — what do you think?"
- "I noticed your work on X was really strong."
- "Can you walk the team through your approach?"

### To Boost Certainty
- "Here's exactly what we know so far..."
- "The timeline is X, and here are the milestones."
- "Even though things are changing, here's what WON'T change."

### To Boost Autonomy
- "How would you like to approach this?"
- "You can choose between A or B — both work for me."
- "I trust your judgment on the implementation details."

### To Boost Relatedness
- "How was your weekend?" (genuine interest)
- "We're all in this together."
- "I had a similar experience — let me share..."

### To Boost Fairness
- "Here's the criteria we'll use to decide."
- "Everyone will have equal time to present their case."
- "I want to be transparent about why this decision was made."

## Red Flags: When SCARF Threats Are Active

| Behavior | Likely Threatened Domain |
|----------|------------------------|
| Arms crossed, monosyllabic answers | Relatedness or Status |
| "That's not fair" / "Why does X get to..." | Fairness |
| Excessive questions about timeline/process | Certainty |
| "I'll do it MY way" / passive resistance | Autonomy |
| Defensive, justifying, name-dropping | Status |
| Withdrawal from group activities | Relatedness |
| Detailed complaints about process | Fairness + Certainty |
| Sudden silence after speaking up | Status (probably just got threatened) |

## Cultural Considerations

SCARF domains are universal, but their relative importance varies by culture:

- **High power-distance cultures** (Korea, Japan): Status and Relatedness are amplified
- **Individualist cultures** (US, Australia): Autonomy is amplified
- **Uncertainty-avoiding cultures** (Germany, Japan): Certainty is amplified
- **Egalitarian cultures** (Scandinavia, Netherlands): Fairness is amplified
- **Collectivist cultures** (Korea, China): Relatedness is amplified, Autonomy is less critical
