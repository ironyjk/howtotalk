# STATE Method — Detailed Guide

> The STATE method from *Crucial Conversations* for sharing tough messages while maintaining safety.

---

## The Five Steps

### S — Share Your Facts (사실을 공유하라)

Facts are the **least controversial** and **most persuasive** elements of your story.

**Why start with facts:**
- Facts are objective and observable — hard to argue with
- Facts are less insulting than conclusions
- Facts create a foundation of shared reality

**How to find your facts:**
1. Separate what you SAW or HEARD from what you CONCLUDED
2. Ask: "Would a neutral third party agree this happened?"
3. Strip away all adjectives that imply judgment

**Examples:**

| Bad (story/judgment) | Good (fact) |
|---|---|
| "You're always late" | "You arrived after the meeting started on Monday, Wednesday, and Thursday this week" |
| "You don't listen to me" | "During our last three conversations, you looked at your phone while I was talking" |
| "Your work quality is slipping" | "The last two reports had 12 and 8 errors respectively, compared to 1-2 in previous months" |
| "You undermined me in the meeting" | "In today's meeting, you presented a different recommendation than the one we discussed yesterday" |

---

### T — Tell Your Story (당신의 해석을 말하라)

After laying the factual foundation, share your **interpretation** — but own it as YOUR story, not THE truth.

**Key principle:** Your story is a HYPOTHESIS, not a verdict.

**Tentative language (잠정적 언어):**
- "I'm beginning to wonder if..."
- "The story I'm telling myself is..."
- "From where I sit, it looks like..."
- "I'm not sure this is right, but..."
- "One possible explanation is..."

**Too soft (loses impact):**
- "I'm probably wrong, but maybe, sort of, it could possibly be..."

**Too hard (kills safety):**
- "It's obvious that you..." / "Clearly you don't..."

**Sweet spot:**
- "I've noticed [fact]. I'm starting to think [story]. I could be wrong — I'd like to hear your take."

---

### A — Ask for Others' Paths (상대방의 관점을 물어라)

Genuinely invite the other person to share their facts AND stories.

**Effective questions:**
- "I'd really like to hear your perspective on this."
- "How do you see this situation?"
- "What am I missing here?"
- "Help me understand what's happening from your side."
- "Do you see it differently?"

**Ineffective questions (loaded / rhetorical):**
- "Don't you think that's wrong?" (leading)
- "How could you possibly justify that?" (attacking)
- "Can you explain yourself?" (interrogating)

**When they share:** LISTEN. Do not reload your argument while they talk. Use AMPP:
- **Ask** to get things rolling
- **Mirror** to confirm emotions ("You seem frustrated")
- **Paraphrase** to acknowledge their story
- **Prime** to offer your best guess if they're stuck

---

### T — Talk Tentatively (잠정적으로 말하라)

This is not about being wishy-washy. It's about acknowledging that your story is YOUR interpretation, not absolute truth.

**Tentative = Confident + Humble**

| Too aggressive | Tentative (good) | Too weak |
|---|---|---|
| "The fact is, you're checking out" | "I'm wondering if maybe you've lost interest — is that what's happening?" | "I don't know, maybe it's nothing, probably just me..." |
| "You obviously don't care" | "From what I can see, it seems like this might not be a priority for you" | "I'm sure you care, forget I said anything" |
| "That's a lie" | "That doesn't match what I recall. Can we look at the data together?" | "Whatever you say, I guess" |

**Why tentative works:**
- Signals that you're open to being wrong
- Invites dialogue rather than debate
- Maintains safety — the other person doesn't need to defend

---

### E — Encourage Testing (검증을 권장하라)

Actively make it safe for the other person to disagree with your story.

**Phrases that encourage testing:**
- "I really want to hear if you see this differently."
- "Please push back if I'm off base."
- "What's wrong with my reasoning?"
- "I'm not looking for agreement — I'm looking for understanding."
- "Is there something I'm not seeing?"

**Body language matters:**
- Lean in, not back
- Open posture
- Genuine eye contact
- Patient silence after asking

**The ultimate test:** If the other person pushes back and you become defensive, you weren't really encouraging testing — you were performing it.

## Complete STATE Examples

### Example 1: Team member missing deadlines

```
S: "Over the past month, three deliverables were submitted after the
    agreed-upon dates — the analysis on March 5 (due March 1), 
    the report on March 15 (due March 10), and the review yesterday
    (due Monday)."

T: "I'm starting to wonder whether something might be going on that's
    making it hard to meet these timelines. I want to make sure I'm
    not assuming something that's not true."

A: "What's your take on this? Is there something I'm not seeing?"

T: "I might be reading this wrong — maybe there are factors I
    don't know about."

E: "I'd genuinely like to understand. If I'm off base, please
    tell me."
```

### Example 2: Business partner making solo decisions

```
S: "Twice this quarter — with the vendor contract and the office 
    relocation — decisions were finalized before we had a chance
    to discuss them together."

T: "The story I'm telling myself is that maybe my input isn't
    valued in these decisions, or that the pace of business is
    making it hard to consult. I honestly don't know which it is."

A: "Help me understand what's happening from your side. 
    How do you see our decision-making process working?"

T: "I could be completely misreading the situation."

E: "If there's a reason these had to move fast that I'm not
    aware of, I really want to hear it."
```

### Example 3: Spouse spending concern

```
S: "I noticed three purchases this month totaling 2.3 million won 
    that weren't in our agreed budget categories."

T: "I'm not saying you're doing anything wrong. I'm feeling 
    anxious because we set savings goals together and I want to
    make sure we're on the same page about our financial plan."

A: "Were these planned purchases? Am I missing context?"

T: "Maybe there are good reasons for each of these that I'm
    not aware of."

E: "I don't want to micromanage — I want us to feel like
    a team. What works for you?"
```

## STATE Method Decision Tree

```
Am I about to share a tough message?
├─ YES → Have I separated FACTS from my STORY?
│        ├─ NO → Go back. Find the camera-testable facts.
│        └─ YES → Am I leading with facts?
│                 ├─ NO → Reorder. Facts first.
│                 └─ YES → Am I sharing my story tentatively?
│                          ├─ NO → Soften. "I'm wondering if..." 
│                          └─ YES → Am I genuinely asking for their view?
│                                   ├─ NO → Add a real question. Mean it.
│                                   └─ YES → Am I encouraging pushback?
│                                            ├─ NO → "Tell me if I'm wrong"
│                                            └─ YES → DELIVER IT.
└─ NO → Is safety broken?
        ├─ YES → Step out. Contrast. Restore safety. Then STATE.
        └─ NO → Continue normal dialogue.
```

---

## Combining STATE with Safety

If at ANY point during STATE the other person goes to silence or violence:

1. **STOP** talking about the content
2. **Restore safety** using Contrasting or CRIB
3. **Resume** STATE from where you left off

**Contrasting template:**
```
"Let me pause. I don't want [what they fear]. 
 What I do want is [your actual purpose]. 
 Can we keep talking about this?"
```
