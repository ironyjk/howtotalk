# Crucial Conversations Skill

> "At the core of every successful conversation lies the free flow of relevant information." — Patterson et al.

## Overview

A Crucial Conversation (결정적 대화) occurs when three conditions collide: (1) stakes are high (이해관계가 크다), (2) opinions differ (의견이 다르다), (3) emotions are strong (감정이 격하다). Most people handle these moments with either silence (침묵) or violence (공격) — both destroy dialogue. This framework teaches how to stay in dialogue when it matters most.

## Core Concept: The Pool of Shared Meaning (공유 의미의 풀)

Every conversation has a "pool" of shared meaning — all the information, ideas, theories, feelings, and opinions that participants share openly. The larger the pool:
- Better decisions (more information available)
- Greater buy-in (people feel heard)
- Stronger commitment (unity of purpose)

**Goal of every crucial conversation:** Keep filling the pool, even when it's hard.

**Two main threats to the pool:**
1. **Silence** (침묵) — Withholding information from the pool
   - Masking: Understating or selectively showing true opinions
   - Avoiding: Steering away from sensitive subjects entirely
   - Withdrawing: Pulling out of the conversation altogether

2. **Violence** (폭력적 대화) — Forcing meaning into the pool
   - Controlling: Coercing others to your way of thinking (cutting off, overstating, speaking absolutes)
   - Labeling: Putting a label on people or ideas to dismiss them
   - Attacking: Moving from winning the argument to making the other person suffer

## Process

### Step 1: Start with Heart (마음에서 시작하라)

Before opening your mouth, get clear on what you REALLY want:

1. **What do I really want for myself?**
2. **What do I really want for the other person?**
3. **What do I really want for the relationship?**
4. **How would I behave if I really wanted these results?**

**The Sucker's Choice (바보의 선택):** The false dilemma that you must choose between honesty and kindness. Refuse it. Ask: "How can I be 100% honest AND 100% respectful?"

### Step 2: Learn to Look (관찰하는 법을 배워라)

Watch for two critical signals during the conversation:

**Signal 1: When a conversation becomes crucial**
- Physical signs: stomach tightening, eyes drying, voice rising
- Emotional signs: feeling scared, hurt, angry
- Behavioral signs: raising voice, going quiet, pointing fingers

**Signal 2: When safety is at risk**
- The other person goes to silence (masking, avoiding, withdrawing)
- The other person goes to violence (controlling, labeling, attacking)
- YOU go to silence or violence

**Dual-processing:** Simultaneously watch the CONTENT and the CONDITIONS (safety level).

### Step 3: Make It Safe (안전하게 만들어라)

When safety breaks down, step OUT of the content and restore safety:

**Two conditions for safety:**
1. **Mutual Purpose (상호 목적)** — Do they believe I care about their goals?
   - If missing → use CRIB:
     - **C**ommit to seek mutual purpose: "I want to find something that works for both of us"
     - **R**ecognize the purpose behind the strategy: What do they REALLY want?
     - **I**nvent a mutual purpose: Find a higher/broader goal you share
     - **B**rainstorm new strategies: With mutual purpose clear, generate options together

2. **Mutual Respect (상호 존중)** — Do they believe I respect them?
   - If missing → Apologize sincerely if you've been disrespectful
   - Find something genuinely respectable about the person
   - Contrast to fix misunderstandings

**Contrasting (대비 기법):**
- "I DON'T want you to think [the negative thing they fear]. I DO want [your actual positive purpose]."
- Example: "I don't want you to think I'm questioning your competence. I do want to find a way we can meet the deadline together."

### Step 4: Master My Stories (나의 이야기를 통제하라)

Between what happens and how we feel, there's a story we tell ourselves:

```
See/Hear → Tell a Story → Feel → Act
(관찰)     (이야기)       (감정)  (행동)
```

**Three "Clever Stories" (교묘한 이야기) that keep us stuck:**
1. **Victim Story (피해자 이야기):** "It's not my fault" — I am innocent, blameless
2. **Villain Story (악당 이야기):** "It's all your fault" — The other person is evil/stupid/wrong
3. **Helpless Story (무력감 이야기):** "There's nothing I can do" — I have no options

**Counter-technique — Turn victims into actors, villains into humans, helpless into able:**
- "What am I pretending not to notice about MY role in this?"
- "Why would a reasonable, rational, decent person do what they did?"
- "What do I really want? What would I do right now to move toward that?"

### Step 5: STATE My Path (STATE 기법으로 말하라)

The core method for sharing tough messages:

| Step | Action | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| **S**hare your facts | Start with the least controversial, most objective elements | "I've noticed you've missed three of our last five 1:1 meetings" |
| **T**ell your story | Explain your interpretation — tentatively | "I'm starting to wonder if these meetings aren't valuable to you" |
| **A**sk for others' paths | Invite the other person's viewpoint | "I'd like to hear how you see it. What's going on?" |
| **T**alk tentatively | Express your story as a story, not a fact | "I could be wrong, but..." / "From my perspective..." |
| **E**ncourage testing | Make it safe for others to push back | "I really want to hear if you see this differently" |

**Key:** Start with FACTS, not stories. Facts are the least controversial starting point and hardest to disagree with.

### Step 6: Explore Others' Paths (상대방의 길을 탐색하라)

When the OTHER person has a story (especially angry/silent):

**AMPP Listening Tools:**
- **A**sk: "What's going on?" / "I'd really like to hear your perspective"
- **M**irror: "You say you're fine, but your tone tells me otherwise"
- **P**araphrase: "So you're saying... Let me make sure I understand..."
- **P**rime (when all else fails): Take your best guess at what they're feeling — "Are you upset because you feel I went over your head?"

**ABC Agreement:**
- **A**gree: Find common ground first
- **B**uild: Add elements they might have missed
- **C**ompare: When you genuinely disagree, compare your views (don't say "wrong")

### Step 7: Move to Action (행동으로 옮겨라)

Dialogue without decision is just talk. Before ending:

1. **Decide HOW to decide:**
   - **Command** (명령): One person decides (appropriate when it's their authority)
   - **Consult** (자문): Gather input, then one person decides
   - **Vote** (투표): Majority rules (when efficiency > quality)
   - **Consensus** (합의): Everyone agrees (highest quality, slowest)

2. **Document the decision:**
   - **Who** does **What** by **When**
   - **How** will we follow up?

## Anti-patterns

1. **Leading with your story, not facts** — "You don't care about this project" vs. "The report was submitted two days late"
2. **Sucker's Choice** — "I can either be honest OR keep the relationship" — refuse this false dilemma
3. **Playing it safe** — Avoiding the conversation entirely because it's uncomfortable
4. **Telling the Villain Story** — Attributing the worst possible motive to the other person
5. **Contrasting incorrectly** — Only using the "I don't want" half without the positive "I do want"
6. **Forcing your path** — Using STATE without genuinely asking for their perspective
7. **Content fixation** — Getting so wrapped up in the topic that you miss safety breaking down
8. **Skipping Move to Action** — Having a great dialogue but leaving without Who/What/When
9. **Premature problem-solving** — Jumping to solutions before both sides feel fully heard
10. **Consensus fetish** — Insisting on consensus when consulting or command would be more appropriate
