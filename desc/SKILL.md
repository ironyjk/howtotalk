# DESC — Assertive Communication Framework

> "Assertiveness is not what you do, it is who you are." — Shakti Gawain

## Overview

DESC is a structured script for assertive communication developed by Sharon and Gordon Bower in *Asserting Yourself* (1976). It provides a clear, repeatable formula for expressing your needs without being passive (doormat) or aggressive (bully).

The framework works because it separates **facts** from **feelings** from **requests** from **outcomes** — preventing the emotional escalation that happens when these get tangled together.

---

## The 4 Steps

### D — Describe (Objective Facts Only)
State what happened or what is happening, using only observable, verifiable facts. No interpretation, no judgment, no emotion.

**Test:** Could a security camera verify this statement?

**Good:** "The report was submitted 3 days after the agreed deadline."
**Bad:** "You're always late with everything." (judgment + generalization)

**Good:** "In the last three meetings, I was interrupted before finishing my point."
**Bad:** "You never let me talk." (generalization + accusation)

**Tips:**
- Use specific dates, times, quantities
- Avoid "always" and "never"
- Describe behavior, not character
- Stick to one incident at a time (don't stack grievances)

### E — Express (Your Feelings and Impact)
Share how the behavior affects you, using I-statements. Own your emotional response rather than blaming it on the other person.

**Formula:** "I feel [emotion] because [impact on you]" or "The impact on me is [specific consequence]"

**Good:** "I felt frustrated because I had to redo the calculations under time pressure."
**Bad:** "You made me angry." (blames them for your emotion)

**Good:** "I'm concerned because the client might lose confidence in our timeline."
**Bad:** "You're ruining our reputation." (accusation, not feeling)

**Tips:**
- Name a specific emotion (frustrated, concerned, disappointed — not "bad" or "upset")
- Connect the feeling to a concrete impact (on you, the team, the project)
- Keep it proportional — don't catastrophize
- One feeling is enough. Don't list five.

### S — Specify (What You Want)
Make a clear, specific, actionable request. Not a vague wish, not a demand — a request.

**Formula:** "I'd like us to..." / "What I'm asking is..." / "Going forward, could we..."

**Good:** "I'd like us to agree on a 48-hour buffer before deadlines so there's time for review."
**Bad:** "I want you to be more responsible." (vague, judgmental)

**Good:** "Could we establish a signal — like raising a hand — for when someone wants to finish their thought?"
**Bad:** "Stop interrupting me." (demand, not request)

**Tips:**
- Be specific enough that both parties can objectively verify whether the request was met
- Focus on future behavior, not past mistakes
- Make it achievable — don't ask for personality transformation
- Frame it as collaboration ("we") when possible

### C — Consequences (Positive Outcomes)
State the positive outcome that will result if the request is honored. This is NOT a threat — it's a vision of the better future.

**Formula:** "That way..." / "The benefit would be..." / "This would help us..."

**Good:** "That way we'd both feel confident in the deliverable quality, and the client stays happy."
**Bad:** "Or else I'll escalate this to management." (threat — last resort only)

**Good:** "This would make our meetings more productive and I think everyone would feel more heard."
**Bad:** "Because if this keeps happening, I'm going to lose it." (threat + emotional escalation)

**Tips:**
- Default to positive consequences (what goes RIGHT)
- Negative consequences are a last resort, stated calmly and factually
- Connect the consequence to shared goals (team success, relationship health)
- If positive consequences don't motivate, you may need to state boundaries — but do so without anger

## The Assertiveness Spectrum

```
PASSIVE ←————————————— ASSERTIVE ——————————————→ AGGRESSIVE
"Whatever you     "Here's what I need,    "Do it my way
 want is fine"      and here's why"         or else"
```

### Passive Communication
- **Behavior:** Avoids conflict, agrees to things they don't want, doesn't express needs
- **Internal experience:** Resentment builds silently
- **Impact on others:** Others don't know what you need; may inadvertently take advantage
- **Body language:** Hunched posture, avoiding eye contact, quiet voice
- **Words:** "It doesn't matter." / "Whatever you think." / "I'm fine." (when not fine)

### Assertive Communication
- **Behavior:** States needs clearly, respects both self and other, open to negotiation
- **Internal experience:** Calm, grounded, honest
- **Impact on others:** Others know where they stand; trust increases
- **Body language:** Upright, steady eye contact, measured voice
- **Words:** "I'd like..." / "I feel... when..." / "Can we find a solution that works for both of us?"

### Aggressive Communication
- **Behavior:** Dominates, blames, intimidates, dismisses others' needs
- **Internal experience:** Anger, righteousness, sometimes fear underneath
- **Impact on others:** Others comply out of fear but disengage emotionally; trust erodes
- **Body language:** Invading space, pointing, raised voice, glaring
- **Words:** "You always..." / "That's stupid." / "I don't care what you think."

### Passive-Aggressive Communication (The Fourth Style)
- **Behavior:** Appears agreeable on the surface, undermines through indirect actions
- **Examples:** Silent treatment, sarcasm, "forgetting" commitments, backhanded compliments
- **Why it happens:** Person feels powerless to be directly assertive, so resistance goes underground

## Supporting Techniques

### I-Statements vs You-Statements

| You-Statement (Blame) | I-Statement (Ownership) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| "You never listen to me." | "I don't feel heard when I'm interrupted." |
| "You're being unreasonable." | "I'm struggling to see how this works for me." |
| "You made me angry." | "I felt angry when that happened." |
| "You should have told me." | "I wish I'd known sooner." |

### Broken Record Technique
Calmly repeat your request without escalation, regardless of the other person's response. Used when someone deflects, argues, or tries to sidetrack.

**Example:**
- "I understand your perspective, and I need the report by Friday."
- "I hear that you're busy, and I need the report by Friday."
- "I appreciate that, and I still need the report by Friday."

**Key:** Same calm tone each time. No escalation. No new arguments. Just the request, repeated.

### Fogging (Partial Agreement)
Agree with the part of a criticism that's true, without accepting the entire frame. Defuses aggression without surrendering your position.

**Example:**
- Attack: "You're too slow with these reports."
- Fogging: "You're right that this one took longer than usual. I'd like to discuss what happened and how to prevent it."

**Example:**
- Attack: "You're always so uptight about rules."
- Fogging: "I do take process seriously. I'm open to discussing which rules feel unnecessary."

### Negative Assertion
When criticism is valid, own it directly. This disarms the attacker because there's nothing left to argue about.

**Example:**
- "You're right, I did miss that deadline. Here's my plan to prevent it next time."

### Negative Inquiry
When criticism is vague or manipulative, ask for specifics. This forces the critic to be concrete (which often deflates vague attacks).

**Example:**
- Attack: "You never contribute in meetings."
- Negative inquiry: "Can you give me a specific example? I'd like to understand what you're seeing."

### `/assert` — Full DESC Script Builder
Build a complete DESC script for a specific situation.

**Process:**
1. Describe the situation, the other person, and your goal
2. Draft each DESC step with specific language
3. Anticipate likely responses and prepare replies
4. Rate assertiveness level and adjust if needed

**Output format:**
```

===========

Context: [situation summary]
Goal: [what you want to achieve]
Relationship: [boss, peer, partner, friend, etc.]

D — DESCRIBE:
"[exact words to say]"

E — EXPRESS:
"[exact words to say]"

S — SPECIFY:
"[exact words to say]"

C — CONSEQUENCES:
"[exact words to say]"

FULL SCRIPT (combined):
"[the complete statement, flowing naturally]"

ANTICIPATED RESPONSES:

If they deflect: "[your reply]"
If they get defensive: "[your reply]"
If they agree: "[your reply]"
If they refuse: "[your boundary statement]"

Assertiveness Check: [Passive / Assertive / Aggressive]
Adjustments: [if needed]
```

### `/assert:desc` — Quick DESC Draft
Rapid-fire DESC script without the full analysis. Just provide the situation and get the 4 lines.

### `/assert:practice` — Role-Play Simulation
Practice assertive communication in a simulated conversation.

**Process:**
1. Choose a scenario:
   - Saying no to extra work
   - Asking for a raise
   - Setting a boundary with a friend
   - Addressing a colleague's behavior
   - Returning a defective product
   - Confronting a recurring issue with a partner
2. AI plays the other person (with realistic pushback)
3. User practices DESC and supporting techniques
4. AI provides feedback after each exchange
5. Scorecard after 5-8 exchanges

**Scorecard metrics:**
- DESC structure adherence (did all 4 steps appear?)
- I-statement usage (ownership vs blame)
- Assertiveness calibration (passive/assertive/aggressive)
- Broken record effectiveness (if needed)
- Emotional regulation (did you escalate?)

### `/assert:diagnose` — Communication Style Diagnosis
Analyze a past conversation or habitual pattern to identify your default communication style and where it breaks down.

**Process:**
1. Describe your typical response in conflict situations
2. Identify patterns (passive? aggressive? passive-aggressive?)
3. Identify triggers (what pushes you from assertive to passive or aggressive?)
4. Create a personal assertiveness development plan

## Common DESC Mistakes

1. **Stacking the D:** Listing 10 past grievances instead of one specific incident. The listener shuts down by item 3.
2. **Skipping the E:** Going straight from facts to demands. Without the emotional connection, the request feels cold or transactional.
3. **Vague S:** "I want things to be better" is not a request. Specify observable behavior.
4. **Threatening C:** Leading with "or else" instead of "so that." Threats trigger defensiveness.
5. **Over-rehearsing:** Sounding robotic. Practice the structure, then let it flow naturally.

---

## When DESC Isn't Enough

- **Power imbalance is extreme:** DESC requires the other person to engage in good faith. With abusive bosses or toxic relationships, you may need to skip straight to boundaries or exit.
- **Cultural context:** In high-context cultures, direct DESC can feel confrontational. Adapt the directness level while keeping the structure.
- **Emergency/safety:** If boundaries are being violated, don't DESC. State the boundary and enforce it.
