# DESC — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creators

DESC was developed by Sharon and Gordon Bower in *Asserting Yourself: A Practical Guide for Positive Change* (1976). It provides a clear, repeatable formula for expressing needs without being passive (doormat) or aggressive (bully). The framework works because it separates **facts** from **feelings** from **requests** from **outcomes** — preventing emotional escalation.

## The Assertiveness Spectrum

```
PASSIVE <-------------- ASSERTIVE --------------> AGGRESSIVE
"Whatever you     "Here's what I need,    "Do it my way
 want is fine"      and here's why"         or else"
```

### Passive Communication
- **Behavior:** Avoids conflict, agrees to things they don't want, doesn't express needs
- **Internal experience:** Resentment builds silently
- **Impact on others:** Others don't know what you need; may inadvertently take advantage
- **Body language:** Hunched posture, avoiding eye contact, quiet voice
- **Words:** "It doesn't matter." / "Whatever you think." / "I'm fine." (when not fine)

### Assertive Communication
- **Behavior:** States needs clearly, respects both self and other, open to negotiation
- **Internal experience:** Calm, grounded, honest
- **Impact on others:** Others know where they stand; trust increases
- **Body language:** Upright, steady eye contact, measured voice
- **Words:** "I'd like..." / "I feel... when..." / "Can we find a solution that works for both of us?"

### Aggressive Communication
- **Behavior:** Dominates, blames, intimidates, dismisses others' needs
- **Internal experience:** Anger, righteousness, sometimes fear underneath
- **Impact on others:** Others comply out of fear but disengage emotionally; trust erodes
- **Body language:** Invading space, pointing, raised voice, glaring
- **Words:** "You always..." / "That's stupid." / "I don't care what you think."

### Passive-Aggressive Communication (The Fourth Style)
- **Behavior:** Appears agreeable on the surface, undermines through indirect actions
- **Examples:** Silent treatment, sarcasm, "forgetting" commitments, backhanded compliments
- **Why it happens:** Person feels powerless to be directly assertive, so resistance goes underground

## Supporting Techniques — Theory

### I-Statements vs You-Statements

| You-Statement (Blame) | I-Statement (Ownership) |
|-----------------------|------------------------|
| "You never listen to me." | "I don't feel heard when I'm interrupted." |
| "You're being unreasonable." | "I'm struggling to see how this works for me." |
| "You made me angry." | "I felt angry when that happened." |
| "You should have told me." | "I wish I'd known sooner." |

### Broken Record Technique
Calmly repeat your request without escalation, regardless of the other person's response. Used when someone deflects, argues, or tries to sidetrack.

**Key:** Same calm tone each time. No escalation. No new arguments. Just the request, repeated.

### Fogging (Partial Agreement)
Agree with the part of a criticism that's true, without accepting the entire frame. Defuses aggression without surrendering your position.

### Negative Assertion
When criticism is valid, own it directly. This disarms the attacker because there's nothing left to argue about.

### Negative Inquiry
When criticism is vague or manipulative, ask for specifics. Forces the critic to be concrete (which often deflates vague attacks).

## When DESC Isn't Enough

- **Power imbalance is extreme:** DESC requires the other person to engage in good faith. With abusive bosses or toxic relationships, you may need to skip straight to boundaries or exit.
- **Cultural context:** In high-context cultures, direct DESC can feel confrontational. Adapt the directness level while keeping the structure.
- **Emergency/safety:** If boundaries are being violated, don't DESC. State the boundary and enforce it.
