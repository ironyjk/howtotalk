# Assertiveness Techniques — Quick Reference

## DESC Script Templates by Situation

### Asking for a Raise

```
D: "I've been in this role for 18 months and have taken on [X, Y, Z] responsibilities
    beyond my original job description."
E: "I feel that my compensation doesn't reflect the scope of what I'm contributing,
    and I want to address that openly."
S: "I'd like to discuss a salary adjustment to [specific number or range] to align
    with my current responsibilities."
C: "This would help me stay fully committed and motivated, and I think it's fair
    given the market rate for this role."
```

### Saying No to Extra Work

```
D: "You've asked me to take on the Henderson account in addition to my current
    three accounts."
E: "I'm concerned that adding a fourth account will impact the quality of work
    on my existing commitments."
S: "I'd like us to discuss either reassigning one of my current accounts or
    adjusting the timeline for Henderson."
C: "That way all four clients get the attention they deserve and nothing falls
    through the cracks."
```

### Addressing a Colleague's Behavior

```
D: "In yesterday's meeting, when I was presenting the Q3 numbers, there were
    three points where I was interrupted before finishing my sentence."
E: "I found it difficult to complete my thoughts, and I felt my contribution
    wasn't being valued."
S: "I'd appreciate if we could agree to let each person finish their point
    before responding."
C: "I think our meetings would be more efficient and everyone would feel
    heard."
```

### Setting a Boundary with a Friend

```
D: "The last three times we made plans, you cancelled within an hour of the
    scheduled time."
E: "I feel disappointed because I rearrange my schedule to make time for us."
S: "Going forward, I'd like us to confirm plans the morning of, and if either
    of us needs to cancel, give at least 4 hours' notice."
C: "That way we can both plan our time better and I won't feel let down."
```

### Returning a Product/Service Complaint

```
D: "I purchased this item on March 5th for $200, and it stopped working after
    two weeks."
E: "I'm frustrated because I expected it to last, and this is within the
    warranty period."
S: "I'd like either a replacement or a full refund."
C: "I've been a customer for three years and I'd like to continue buying
    from your store."
```

### Confronting a Partner

```
D: "When I share something about my day and the response is 'uh-huh' while
    scrolling your phone..."
E: "I feel like what I'm saying isn't important to you, and it makes me
    pull back from sharing."
S: "Could we set aside 15 minutes after dinner with no screens, just to
    talk about our days?"
C: "I think we'd both feel more connected, and I'd love to hear about
    your day too."
```

## Broken Record — Advanced Usage

### When They Deflect
- Them: "But what about the time YOU were late?"
- You: "I'm happy to discuss that separately. Right now, I'm asking about the deadline."

### When They Minimize
- Them: "You're making a big deal out of nothing."
- You: "It may seem small, and it matters to me. I'd like us to address it."

### When They Guilt-Trip
- Them: "After everything I've done for you, this is how you repay me?"
- You: "I appreciate what you've done. And I still need us to address this."

### When They Escalate
- Them: (raising voice) "This is ridiculous!"
- You: (calm, lower voice) "I can see you're upset. I'd like to continue this conversation when we're both calm. My request remains the same."

## Fogging — Pattern Library

| Attack | Fogging Response |
|--------|-----------------|
| "You're too sensitive." | "I am responsive to how people treat me. Here's what I need..." |
| "You're just like your mother." | "I do share some traits with her. Let's get back to the issue." |
| "You don't know what you're talking about." | "I might be missing something. Can you help me understand your perspective?" |
| "Everyone else is fine with it." | "That may be true. I'm sharing how it affects me specifically." |
| "You're overthinking this." | "I do think things through carefully. Here's the specific concern..." |

## Body Language for Assertiveness

### DO
- Stand or sit upright (not stiff, but grounded)
- Maintain steady eye contact (look at the bridge of their nose if direct eye contact is hard)
- Keep hands visible and relaxed (not in pockets, not crossed)
- Speak at moderate pace and volume
- Use pauses deliberately (shows confidence)
- Breathe from diaphragm (prevents voice from shaking)

### DON'T
- Smile when delivering a serious message (sends mixed signals)
- Touch your face/hair (signals anxiety)
- Shift weight back and forth (signals wanting to flee)
- Cross arms (signals defensiveness)
- Point or jab finger (signals aggression)
- Look down while speaking (signals submission)

## The Assertiveness Bill of Rights

From Manuel Smith's *When I Say No, I Feel Guilty*:

1. You have the right to judge your own behavior, thoughts, and emotions.
2. You have the right to offer no reasons or excuses for justifying your behavior.
3. You have the right to judge whether you are responsible for finding solutions to other people's problems.
4. You have the right to change your mind.
5. You have the right to make mistakes — and be responsible for them.
6. You have the right to say "I don't know."
7. You have the right to be independent of the goodwill of others before coping with them.
8. You have the right to be illogical in making decisions.
9. You have the right to say "I don't understand."
10. You have the right to say "I don't care."
