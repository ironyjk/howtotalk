# Digital Communication Adaptation Guide

> "The medium is the message." — Marshall McLuhan

When communication frameworks were developed, most assumed face-to-face interaction. This guide adapts them for digital channels where tone, timing, and bandwidth change everything.

---

## Channel Characteristics

### Text (카톡 / 슬랙 / 이메일)

- **No tone of voice** — Words carry 100% of the meaning. Sarcasm, warmth, and urgency are invisible without deliberate markers.
- **Emoji/이모티콘 as tone markers** — A period at the end of "OK." feels cold. "OK!" or "OK 👍" feels warm. Emoji are not decoration; they are tone.
- **Read receipts as pressure** — Seeing "read" without a reply creates anxiety. Be aware that read receipts add implicit urgency.
- **Asynchronous = time to think** — This is a superpower. You can draft, revise, and think before sending. Use it.
- **Permanence** — Text messages are screenshots waiting to happen. Never write what you wouldn't want forwarded.

### Video Call (Zoom / Teams / Google Meet)

- **Camera fatigue** — Staring at faces (including your own) for hours drains energy. Normalize cameras-off for long sessions.
- **Muted = invisible** — When muted with camera off, you effectively don't exist. Active participation requires deliberate effort.
- **Chat sidebar as parallel channel** — The chat box is a second conversation happening simultaneously. Monitor it or designate someone to.
- **Screen sharing shifts power** — The person sharing controls attention. Be deliberate about when to share and when to stop.
- **Breakout rooms** — Digital equivalent of "let's step outside for a moment." Use for difficult 1:1 within group contexts.

### Voice Call (전화)

- **Tone of voice available, body language not** — You can hear emotion but can't see it. Listen harder.
- **Silence is ambiguous** — In person, silence with eye contact = attentive. On phone, silence = "Are you still there?"
- **Walking meetings** — Phone calls while walking can reduce confrontation and increase creativity.

## Framework Adaptations

### NVC via Text

Extra careful with **observation** (Step 1). Without tone context, "When you sent that email at 11pm" can read as accusation even when intended as neutral observation. Add explicit framing: "I want to share something — this isn't a criticism, just what I noticed..."

- Feelings via text: Use explicit emotion words. "I felt frustrated" is clearer than "😤" (which could be read as anger, annoyance, or sarcasm).
- Requests via text: Be extra specific. "Could you..." is ambiguous about urgency. Add timeframe: "Could you by Thursday?"

### Active Listening via Text

- **"I'm reading carefully"** replaces nodding
- **Paraphrasing** works even better in text — quote their exact words back
- **"Tell me more about that"** signals engagement when you can't lean forward
- **Response time matters** — A thoughtful 5-minute delay can signal "I'm taking this seriously" or "I don't care." Disambiguate: "Give me a few minutes to think about this properly."

### Never Split the Difference via Email

- **Labeling works great in writing** — "It seems like..." and "It sounds like..." are natural in email and give the reader time to reflect
- **Mirroring** — Repeat their last 3 words or key phrase. Works in text just as well as in speech.
- **Calibrated questions** — Email is perfect for "How am I supposed to do that?" — it doesn't sound confrontational in writing the way it might in person.
- **The late-night FM DJ voice** — Obviously doesn't apply. Use measured, calm sentence structure instead.

### DESC via Text

Actually **easier** in text — you can draft each step carefully:
- **D**escribe: Quote specific messages/actions (you have the receipts)
- **E**xpress: Write and revise your feeling statement before sending
- **S**pecify: Be precise about what you want (no ambiguity)
- **C**onsequences: Reads more formally in text — soften with "I hope we can..."

### Crucial Conversations via Zoom

- **Harder to read safety signals** — You can't see crossed arms, you might miss a frown on a small thumbnail
- **Use explicit safety checks**: "How are you feeling about this conversation so far?"
- **Contrasting statements** are essential: "I don't want you to think I'm criticizing your work. I do want to find a way to hit our deadline."
- **Chat DM** — If someone goes silent in a group call, DM them privately: "I noticed you got quiet — everything OK?"

### Storytelling via Slides/Screen Share

- **Sparkline** translates perfectly to slide decks — alternate between "What Is" and "What Could Be" slides
- **STAR** works well in written case studies and LinkedIn posts
- **기승전결** — Natural fit for Korean business emails and reports
- **두괄식** — The default for Slack/Teams messages: lead with the conclusion, support below

### Influence in Digital Contexts

- **Social Proof** is amplified online (reviews, follower counts, likes)
- **Scarcity** is harder to verify online — skepticism is warranted
- **Authority** — Credentials are easier to fake digitally. Verify.
- **Unity** — Harder to build through screens; prioritize video over text for relationship-building

## Key Principle

**"When in doubt, move to higher-bandwidth channel."**

```
Text (카톡/슬랙) → Voice call → Video call → In-person
     Lowest                                    Highest
     bandwidth                                 bandwidth
```

- Misunderstanding in text? → Pick up the phone.
- Emotions running high on a call? → Turn on video.
- Video call going nowhere? → "Can we meet in person for this?"
- The rule: **escalate the channel before escalating the conflict.**

### When to Stay in Text

Not every conversation needs to be "upgraded." Stay in text when:
- The topic is factual/logistical (scheduling, data sharing)
- You need a written record
- The other person needs time to process (introverts, ESL speakers, emotional topics)
- You want to be precise and avoid being put on the spot
- 두괄식 delivery: conclusion + supporting points, no discussion needed

### When to Move to Voice/Video

Move up when:
- You've exchanged more than 3 messages without resolution
- Tone is being misread (or you suspect it is)
- The topic involves emotions, feedback, or conflict
- You need real-time collaboration or brainstorming
- The relationship matters more than the content

## Cross-Cultural Digital Norms

| Norm | Korea (한국) | US/Western | Note |
|------|-------------|------------|------|
| Response speed | Fast expected (특히 카톡) | Async OK | Korean 카톡 culture expects quick replies |
| Emoji use | Heavy, expressive | Moderate | Korean 이모티콘 carry nuance |
| Video cameras | Often off in large meetings | Expected on in some cultures | Ask, don't assume |
| After-hours messages | Common, expected to read | Can be seen as boundary violation | Know your audience |
| Voice messages | Common (카톡 voice) | Less common | Bridging text and call |
