# Cognitive Bias Mapping to Communication Frameworks

> When someone's thinking is distorted by a cognitive bias, arguing with their conclusion is futile. You need to address the **thinking process** itself. Each bias has a framework that's particularly effective at disrupting it.

---

## Bias-to-Framework Map

| Cognitive Bias (인지 편향) | What It Sounds Like | Best Framework | Why |
|--------------------------|---------------------|----------------|-----|
| **Fundamental Attribution Error** (근본 귀인 오류) | "넌 원래 그런 사람이야" / "He's just lazy" | NVC + Difficult Conversations | Separate observation from evaluation; shift from blame to contribution. NVC forces "When you did X" instead of "You ARE X". Difficult Conversations explores the contribution system rather than assigning character flaws. |
| **Confirmation Bias** (확증 편향) | "내 말이 맞잖아, 봐" / "See, I told you so" | Socratic Method | Questions that challenge evidence quality: "What would change your mind?" "What evidence would look like if the opposite were true?" Don't argue the conclusion — question the evidence-gathering process. |
| **False Consensus** (합의 편향) | "다들 그렇게 생각해" / "Everyone agrees with me" | Calibrated Questions (Never Split) | "Who specifically thinks that?" "How many people have you actually asked?" "What did [specific person] say when you asked them directly?" Forces them to move from vague consensus to concrete evidence. |
| **Availability Heuristic** (가용성 휴리스틱) | "한번 해봤는데 안 됐어" / "We tried that once and it failed" | Motivational Interviewing | Explore beyond the one bad experience. "Tell me about that time. What was different then? What might be different now?" Roll with the resistance rather than dismissing their experience. |
| **Dunning-Kruger Effect** | "나는 이미 다 알아" / "I've got this figured out" | Socratic Method | Questions that reveal knowledge gaps gently: "How would you handle [specific edge case]?" "Walk me through what happens when [complication]?" The person discovers their own gaps rather than being told. |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** (매몰비용) | "여기까지 왔는데 포기 못해" / "We've invested too much to quit now" | Getting to Yes (BATNA) | Focus on future value, not past investment. "Forgetting everything we've spent so far — if we were starting fresh today, would we choose this path?" BATNA analysis reveals whether continuing actually serves their interests. |
| **Anchoring Bias** (앵커링) | First number dominates the negotiation | Never Split (Ackerman) | Counter-anchor strategically. Use the Ackerman model (65-85-95-100%) to systematically move away from their anchor. Or pre-empt with your own anchor. Label the anchor: "It sounds like you've got a number in mind." |
| **Projection** (투사) | "그건 네 문제야" / "YOU'RE the one who's angry" | NVC + Active Listening | Reflect without defending. Don't counter-attack ("No, YOU'RE projecting"). Instead: "It sounds like you're sensing some tension. Can you tell me more about what you're experiencing?" Let them hear their own projection reflected back. |
| **Rationalization** (합리화) | "어쩔 수 없었어" / "I had no choice" | Socratic Method | "What would you do differently if you could go back?" "What options did you consider before choosing this one?" "If a friend came to you with the same situation, what would you advise?" Gently tests the logic without attacking it. |
| **Black-and-White Thinking** (흑백사고) | "넌 내 편이야 아니야" / "You're either with me or against me" | Difficult Conversations (Identity) | Embrace complexity with both/and thinking. The Identity Conversation teaches that you can be BOTH loyal AND disagree. "I can care about you deeply AND see this differently. Both are true." |

## Additional Common Biases

| Cognitive Bias | What It Sounds Like | Best Framework | Why |
|----------------|---------------------|----------------|-----|
| **Status Quo Bias** (현상유지 편향) | "지금도 잘 되고 있잖아" | Motivational Interviewing | Explore the discrepancy between current state and their stated values/goals. Don't push change — evoke their own dissatisfaction. |
| **Negativity Bias** (부정 편향) | "한 번 실수하면 끝이야" | Radical Candor + Active Listening | Acknowledge the negative (don't dismiss it), then provide specific, concrete positive evidence using SBI format. |
| **Self-Serving Bias** (자기 본위 편향) | "성공은 내 덕, 실패는 남 탓" | Difficult Conversations (Contribution) | Shift from blame to contribution system. "What did each of us contribute to this outcome?" |
| **Bandwagon Effect** (편승 효과) | "요즘 다 그렇게 해" | Socratic Method | "Is it working well for them? How would you measure that? What's different about our situation?" |
| **Halo Effect** (후광 효과) | "그 사람이 하면 다 잘 될 거야" | Socratic Method + Getting to Yes | Separate the person from the proposal. "Let's evaluate this idea on its own merits. What objective criteria should we use?" |

## How to Recognize When YOU Are the One with the Bias

This is the hardest part. Biases are invisible to the person holding them. Use these self-awareness checks:

### Physical Signals
- **Certainty feels like relief.** If you feel suddenly certain and relieved, that's often your brain finding a comfortable story, not the truth.
- **Defensive tension.** If your body tenses when someone challenges your view, ask: "Am I defending a conclusion or defending my ego?"

### Thinking Signals
- **"Obviously..."** — When you prefix a thought with "obviously" or "clearly," it usually means you haven't examined it.
- **Cherry-picking.** Notice if you're seeking evidence that confirms what you already believe.
- **Dismissing disconfirming evidence.** "That's an exception" or "That doesn't count" are red flags.
- **Speed of judgment.** The faster you reach a conclusion about someone's character or a situation, the more likely bias is involved.

### Behavioral Signals
- **You only ask people who agree with you.** If your "research" only involves people who share your view, that's False Consensus.
- **You can't steelman the other side.** If you can't articulate the strongest version of the opposing argument, you haven't understood it.
- **You're using "always" and "never."** These are almost always (!) signs of overgeneralization.

### The 3-Question Self-Check

Before any important decision or conversation, ask yourself:

1. **"What would I need to see to change my mind?"** If the answer is "nothing," you're not reasoning — you're defending.
2. **"What am I afraid of being true?"** The thing you most resist considering is often the thing most worth examining.
3. **"Would I judge someone else the same way in this situation?"** If not, you're likely operating under Self-Serving Bias.

### Practice: The Bias Audit

After any significant conversation or decision:
- Write down the conclusion you reached
- List the evidence that supported it
- List the evidence that contradicted it
- If the second list is empty, go find some — it exists

## Using This Map in Practice

### Step 1: Identify the Bias
Listen for the telltale phrases in the "What It Sounds Like" column. Don't label the bias out loud — saying "You have confirmation bias" triggers defensiveness.

### Step 2: Select the Framework
Use the recommended framework from the table. Load the full skill if needed for detailed techniques.

### Step 3: Apply Indirectly
The goal is never to "fix" someone's bias. The goal is to create conditions where they can see more clearly on their own. Every recommended framework above works by **expanding the person's perspective**, not by arguing against their current one.

### Step 4: Be Patient
Cognitive biases are deeply wired. One conversation won't eliminate them. But consistent application of the right framework, over time, helps people develop better thinking habits.
