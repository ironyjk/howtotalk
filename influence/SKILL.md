# Influence — The Psychology of Persuasion Skill

> "There is a natural human tendency to dislike a person who brings us unpleasant information, even when that person did not cause the bad news. The simple association with it is enough to stimulate our dislike." — Robert Cialdini

## Overview

Robert Cialdini's research identifies seven universal principles (7가지 설득 원칙) that drive human compliance. These mental shortcuts evolved for good reasons — they help us navigate a complex world efficiently. But they can also be exploited. This skill teaches ethical application AND defense against manipulation.

## The 7 Principles of Influence

### Principle 1: Reciprocity (상호성)

**How it works:** When someone gives us something, we feel compelled to give something back. This is deeply wired — societies that developed reciprocity survived; those that didn't, collapsed.

**Mechanism:**
- A gift, favor, concession, or even information creates an obligation
- The obligation feels uncomfortable until discharged
- People will often give back MORE than they received
- Uninvited first favors also create obligation

**Ethical use:**
- Be the first to give — provide value, information, or concessions genuinely
- In negotiation: make a meaningful concession first → they'll feel compelled to reciprocate
- In business: free samples, valuable content, helpful introductions
- In relationships: be generous without keeping score (but not as a manipulation)

**Rejection-then-retreat (거절 후 양보) technique:**
1. Make a large request (likely to be rejected)
2. Follow with a smaller request (your actual goal)
3. The smaller request looks like a concession → triggers reciprocity
- Example: "Could you volunteer 10 hours/week? No? How about 2 hours this Saturday?"

**Defense against manipulation:**
- Accept genuine gifts graciously, but recognize when a "gift" is a sales tactic
- Ask yourself: "If this gift came with no strings, would I still want to comply?"
- Redefine the situation: "This is a sales technique, not a gift"

### Principle 2: Commitment & Consistency (일관성)

**How it works:** Once we take a stand or make a choice, we encounter personal and interpersonal pressure to behave consistently with that commitment. We want to appear (and be) consistent with what we've already done.

**Mechanism:**
- Small commitments lead to larger ones (foot-in-the-door / 문전 걸치기)
- Public commitments are stronger than private ones
- Written commitments are stronger than verbal ones
- Active commitments (choosing to do) are stronger than passive ones
- Commitments we believe are freely chosen are strongest of all

**Ethical use:**
- Start with small asks that align with your eventual larger request
- Get public commitment: "Would you say you value [X]?"
- Written goals: Have people write down their intentions
- In management: Let people set their own targets → they'll work harder to meet them
- In sales: "You mentioned quality is your top priority..." → link your offer to their stated value

**The lowball technique (미끼 기법):**
1. Get commitment to an attractive deal
2. Reveal hidden costs or remove the attractive feature
3. Person still feels committed (consistency pressure)
- Unethical when used deceptively; ethical when circumstances genuinely change

**Defense against manipulation:**
- "Foolish consistency is the hobgoblin of little minds" — Emerson
- Ask: "Knowing what I know now, would I make this same choice?"
- Separate the original decision from the current situation
- It's okay to change your mind when new information arrives

---

### Principle 3: Social Proof (사회적 증거)

**How it works:** We determine what is correct by observing what other people think is correct. The greater the uncertainty, the more we look to others.

**Mechanism:**
- We follow what similar others do (similarity matters)
- Uncertainty amplifies the effect — "When in doubt, follow the crowd"
- Largest effect when the proof comes from people like us (동질 집단)
- Numbers matter — "5 million customers can't be wrong"

**Ethical use:**
- Testimonials from similar customers/users
- Case studies showing others in comparable situations
- "Most people in your industry choose..." (if true)
- Show adoption rates: "85% of our clients renew"
- In leadership: highlight team members who exemplify desired behavior
- In persuasion: "Other parents in this school district have found..."

**Conditions that amplify social proof:**
1. **Uncertainty** — the less sure we are, the more we follow others
2. **Similarity** — we follow people who are LIKE us
3. **Numbers** — more people = stronger proof
4. **Expertise** — social proof from experts carries extra weight

**Defense against manipulation:**
- Fake reviews, inflated numbers, manufactured consensus
- Ask: "Is this social proof genuine? From people truly like me?"
- Beware of "pluralistic ignorance" — everyone follows the group, but nobody actually agrees
- In emergencies: don't wait for others to act (bystander effect / 방관자 효과)

---

### Principle 4: Authority (권위)

**How it works:** We defer to experts and authority figures — often automatically, based on symbols of authority rather than actual expertise.

**Mechanism:**
- Titles (Dr., Professor, CEO) trigger automatic deference
- Uniforms and clothing (white coat, suit, badge)
- Trappings of success (expensive car, nice office, credentials on the wall)
- Expertise in one domain gets generalized to others (halo effect)

**Ethical use:**
- Establish genuine expertise before making your case
- Reference credible sources and research
- Introduce credentials naturally (not boastfully)
- Have a third party introduce your qualifications
- In business: display relevant certifications, experience, publications
- In persuasion: cite experts who support your position

**The vulnerability admission (취약점 인정):**
Paradoxically, admitting a weakness INCREASES perceived authority:
- "I'm not an expert in X, but in my 20 years of Y..."
- "There are downsides to our approach — specifically..."
- Honesty about negatives makes positives more credible

**Defense against manipulation:**
- Ask: "Is this person a genuine expert in THIS specific area?"
- Titles can be fake, uniforms can be costumes, credentials can be irrelevant
- Two questions: (1) "Is this authority truly an expert?" (2) "How truthful can I expect them to be?"
- Authority + conflict of interest = be skeptical

---

### Principle 5: Liking (호감)

**How it works:** We prefer to say yes to people we know and like. Several factors influence liking, and they can be triggered systematically.

**Factors that drive liking:**

| Factor | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Physical attractiveness (외모) | Halo effect — attractive = good | Good-looking salespeople sell more |
| Similarity (유사성) | We like people who are like us | Same hometown, same hobbies, same values |
| Compliments (칭찬) | Even obvious flattery often works | "You clearly have great taste" |
| Contact & Cooperation (접촉+협력) | Familiarity and working together | Joint projects build bonds |
| Conditioning & Association (연합) | We like messengers of good news | Sports fans say "WE won" but "THEY lost" |

**Ethical use:**
- Find genuine common ground — shared interests, backgrounds, values
- Give sincere compliments (not manipulative flattery)
- Build rapport before making requests
- Cooperate before competing — work on a shared problem first
- Associate your message with things the audience already likes
- In sales/leadership: be genuinely interested in the other person

**The Tupperware party model:** Friends sell to friends. The product matters less than the relationship. This is powerful — and can be exploitive.

**Defense against manipulation:**
- Separate the person from the proposal: "Do I like the DEAL, or do I like the PERSON?"
- Watch for artificial rapport-building (mirroring your body language, "What a coincidence, I also...")
- Be extra cautious when you find yourself liking someone unusually quickly

### Principle 6: Scarcity (희소성)

**How it works:** Opportunities seem more valuable to us when their availability is limited. We fear loss more than we desire gain (loss aversion / 손실 회피).

**Mechanism:**
- "Limited time offer" / "Only 3 left in stock"
- Deadline pressure: "Offer expires Friday"
- Exclusive information: "I'm not supposed to tell you this, but..."
- Competition: "Another buyer is interested"
- Psychological reactance: When freedom is threatened, we want it MORE (청소년 반항)

**Two conditions that amplify scarcity:**
1. **Newly scarce** — Something we COULD have but might lose is more motivating than something we never had
2. **Competition** — Scarcity + competitors wanting the same thing = maximum urgency

**Ethical use:**
- Genuine deadlines: "The pricing structure changes next quarter" (if true)
- Honest availability: "We can only take 3 more clients this month" (if true)
- Exclusive insights: Share genuinely rare information
- In negotiation: Make your offer time-sensitive (but only if the constraint is real)

**Defense against manipulation:**
- Artificial urgency is the oldest trick in sales
- Ask: "Do I want this because of its utility, or because I might lose it?"
- Walk away and wait 24 hours. If it's gone, there will be another.
- "A thing is not more valuable just because it's scarce"

### Principle 7: Unity (일체감) — *Added in Pre-Suasion (2016)*

**How it works:** We say yes to people who are "one of us" — not just similar (liking), but belonging to the same identity group. Shared identity ("우리") is the deepest form of influence.

**Mechanism:**
- Family and kinship: "We're family"
- Place and locality: "We're from the same hometown"
- Profession/Vocation: "We're both engineers"
- Shared adversity: "We went through that together"
- Co-creation: Working together on something creates unity

**Types of unity:**
1. **Being together** — Shared category membership (같은 집단)
2. **Acting together** — Synchronized activity, co-creation, shared rituals

**Ethical use:**
- Emphasize genuine shared identity: "As fellow parents..." / "As people in this industry..."
- Create opportunities for co-creation — involve people in building the solution
- Use "we" language naturally (not manipulatively)
- Shared rituals and experiences build lasting bonds
- In leadership: "We're all in this together" (backed by actions, not just words)

**Defense against manipulation:**
- "We" can be manufactured to create false intimacy
- Nationalism, tribalism, and in-group pressure can override rational judgment
- Ask: "Am I agreeing because this makes sense, or because they're 'one of us'?"

## Pre-Suasion (사전 설득)

Cialdini's 2016 follow-up introduced the concept that what happens BEFORE the persuasion attempt matters as much as the attempt itself.

**Core idea:** The moment before you deliver your message is the moment of greatest influence. What you do in that moment — what you draw attention to — shapes how your message is received.

**Key pre-suasion tactics:**
- **Attention = Importance:** What people focus on, they consider important
- **Priming (점화 효과):** Exposure to a concept makes related concepts more accessible
- **Anchoring (앵커링):** The first number/idea sets the reference point
- **Environmental cues:** Physical warmth → warmth toward others; heavy clipboard → serious evaluation
- **Questions as focus tools:** "Do you consider yourself adventurous?" → person now thinks of adventurous self → more likely to try something new

## Anti-patterns

1. **Using scarcity dishonestly** — "Only 2 left!" when there are 200. Destroys trust permanently.
2. **Fake authority** — Citing irrelevant credentials or fabricated expertise.
3. **Manufactured social proof** — Fake reviews, bot followers, paid testimonials. Increasingly detectable and legally risky.
4. **Exploitation of reciprocity** — Giving "gifts" solely to create obligation, with no genuine generosity.
5. **Manipulative unity claims** — "We're family" while treating people as expendable.
6. **Stacking all 7 principles at once** — Feels overwhelming and manipulative. Choose 2-3 that fit naturally.
7. **Ignoring ethics for effectiveness** — Short-term gains from manipulation destroy long-term relationships and reputation.
8. **Not recognizing when YOU'RE being influenced** — The principles work on everyone, including you. Self-awareness is the first defense.
9. **Confusing liking with agreement** — Being charming is not the same as being right.
10. **Over-relying on a single principle** — Different audiences respond to different principles. Adapt.

## The Ethical Framework

Cialdini himself emphasizes: these tools should be used to **point people toward genuine value**, not to trick them into bad decisions.

**Three tests for ethical use:**
1. **Truth test:** Is the information I'm presenting accurate?
2. **Value test:** Will the other person genuinely benefit from what I'm asking?
3. **Relationship test:** Would I be comfortable if they knew exactly what I was doing and why?

If all three are yes: use the principles freely.
If any is no: reconsider.

## Persuasion vs Manipulation: The Line (설득 vs 조작의 경계)

| Situation | Persuasion (OK) | Manipulation (NOT OK) |
|-----------|----------------|----------------------|
| Scarcity | "Only 3 spots left" (true) | "Only 3 spots left" (actually 300) |
| Social Proof | "80% of customers chose this" (verified) | Cherry-picked fake reviews |
| Authority | Citing real credentials | Fake titles, borrowed credibility |
| Reciprocity | Genuine free sample | "Free" with hidden obligations |
| Liking | Finding real common ground | Faking interests to build rapport |
| Commitment | "You said X, does that still apply?" | Bait-and-switch after commitment |

**The Transparency Test**: If the other person knew exactly what technique you were using, would they still be OK with it? If yes → persuasion. If no → manipulation.
