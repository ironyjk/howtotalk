> From Robert Cialdini's *Influence* (2021) and *Pre-Suasion* (2016)

## Principle 1: Reciprocity (상호성) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
People feel obligated to return favors, gifts, invitations, and concessions.

### Classic Research
- **Hare Krishna flower technique:** Give a flower → ask for donation. Worked even when people didn't want the flower.
- **Dennis Regan (1971):** "Joe" buys subject a Coke → subject buys twice as many raffle tickets as control group. Effect worked even when subjects didn't LIKE Joe.
- **Ethiopian Red Cross (1985):** Ethiopia, in the midst of famine, sent aid to Mexico after a 1985 earthquake. Why? Mexico had sent aid to Ethiopia in 1935. Fifty years of reciprocity debt.

### Application Tactics

**Give first, give unexpectedly, give personally:**
| Element | Weak | Strong |
|---|---|---|
| Timing | After they help you | Before you ask anything |
| Expectation | Expected/routine | Surprising/unexpected |
| Personalization | Generic | Tailored to their specific situation |

**Restaurant tip study (Strohmetz et al., 2002):**
- Mints with the check: +3% tips
- Two mints: +14% tips
- One mint, walk away, come back and say "For you nice people, an extra mint": +23% tips
- The personal, unexpected nature of the third approach triggered maximum reciprocity

**Concession reciprocity (rejection-then-retreat):**
```
Large request → "No" → Smaller request → "Yes"

The retreat IS the gift. Your concession obligates their concession.
```

### Business Applications
- Free trials, samples, valuable content (lead magnets)
- Do favors for clients BEFORE asking for referrals
- In proposals: offer something valuable beyond what was asked
- In salary negotiation: demonstrate extra value delivered BEFORE asking for a raise

## Principle 2: Commitment & Consistency (일관성) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
People want to be (and appear) consistent with their prior commitments and self-image.

### Classic Research
- **Freedman & Fraser (1966) — Foot-in-the-door:** Small sign in window → 4x more likely to accept HUGE sign on lawn later
- **Moriarty (1975) — Beach blanket:** Asked stranger to "watch my things" → 19/20 chased a staged thief. Without the request: 4/20.
- **Cialdini (1978) — Lowball:** Agreed to wake up for 7am experiment → still showed up after told it was actually 7am (not 9am as originally implied)
- **Chinese POW camps (Korean War):** Got prisoners to write small pro-Communist statements → gradually escalated → genuine belief change

### The Power Hierarchy of Commitments

| Type | Strength | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Internal (thought only) | Weakest | "I think I should exercise more" |
| Verbal (said aloud) | Moderate | "I told my friend I'd start running" |
| Written | Strong | "I wrote my goal on paper" |
| Public | Stronger | "I posted my goal on social media" |
| Effortful | Strongest | "I ran 5K at 6am in the rain" |

**Most powerful:** Public + Written + Effortful + Freely chosen

### Application Tactics
- **Start small:** "Would you agree that quality matters?" → later: "As someone who values quality..."
- **Get it in writing:** Meeting notes, emails confirming agreement, signed goals
- **Public declarations:** Team commitments in meetings, stated values
- **Ask for identity statements:** "Are you the type of person who ___?"

## Principle 3: Social Proof (사회적 증거) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
When uncertain, people look to similar others to determine the correct behavior.

### Classic Research
- **Milgram (1969) — Looking up:** 1 person looks up → 4% of passersby look up. 15 people → 40% look up.
- **Hotel towel reuse:** "Most guests reuse their towels" → 26% increase. "Most guests IN THIS ROOM reuse" → 33% increase. Specificity and similarity amplify the effect.
- **Laugh tracks:** Work even when we KNOW they're fake. Increase perceived humor and enjoyment.
- **Suicide contagion (Werther effect):** Publicized suicides lead to increase in similar suicides. The more similar the publicized victim to the reader, the stronger the effect.

### Conditions That Maximize Social Proof

| Condition | Effect | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Uncertainty (불확실성) | Amplifies dramatically | New employee follows whatever coworkers do |
| Similarity (유사성) | Strongest influence | "Other CEOs in manufacturing chose..." |
| Numbers (수량) | Adds credibility | "Join 2 million users" |
| Proximity (근접성) | More relevant = more powerful | "Your neighbor just installed solar" |
| Recency (최신성) | Recent proof > old proof | "Last month, 500 companies signed up" |

### Application Tactics
- Use testimonials from people LIKE your audience (same industry, size, situation)
- Show adoption numbers and trends
- Highlight what the MAJORITY does (not the minority who don't)
- User stories > feature lists
- In leadership: spotlight desired behaviors publicly

### The Dark Side
- **Pluralistic ignorance:** Everyone looks around, nobody acts (emergency situations)
- **Bandwagon effect:** People adopt beliefs because others hold them
- **Manufactured social proof:** Fake reviews, bot armies, astroturfing

## Principle 4: Authority (권위) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
People defer to experts and authority figures, often automatically based on symbols rather than substance.

### Classic Research
- **Milgram obedience (1963):** 65% of participants delivered maximum "shocks" when instructed by an authority figure
- **Hofling nurse study (1966):** 95% of nurses administered an overdose when instructed by phone by an unknown "doctor"
- **Bickman (1974):** Person in security guard uniform → 92% compliance with "pick up that bag." Civilian clothes → 42%.

### Three Symbols of Authority

| Symbol | Example | How It's Exploited |
|---|---|---|
| Titles (직함) | Dr., Professor, CEO, General | Fake or irrelevant titles |
| Clothing (복장) | White coat, suit, uniform | Costume ≠ competence |
| Trappings (장식) | Expensive car, fancy office, awards | Wealth ≠ expertise |

### Application Tactics
- **Establish expertise early** but naturally — have others introduce your credentials
- **Admit weaknesses first** — counterintuitive but increases credibility: "Our product isn't the cheapest, but..."
- **Cite genuine authorities** — research, experts, institutions
- **Display relevant credentials** — key word: RELEVANT
- **Earned media > paid media** — being quoted by others > claiming expertise yourself

### The Vulnerability Admission Strategy
1. Mention a weakness first (small, real, but manageable)
2. Transition with "but" or "however"
3. State your strength (the thing you actually want them to believe)
- "We're a small firm [weakness], but that means every partner personally handles your account [strength]"
- Listeners perceive you as more honest → believe your strengths more

## Principle 5: Liking (호감) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
We comply more readily with people we like.

### The Five Liking Factors

**1. Physical Attractiveness (외적 매력)**
- Halo effect: Attractive people are assumed to be smarter, kinder, more honest
- Effect on: hiring, salary, court verdicts, elections
- Not something you can fake, but grooming and presentation matter

**2. Similarity (유사성)**
- We like people who are like us in: opinions, personality, background, lifestyle
- Name-letter effect: We even prefer letters in our own name
- Salespeople trained to "find common ground" — because it works

**3. Compliments (칭찬)**
- Even when we suspect flattery, it still works
- We like people who like us (reciprocal liking)
- Sincere > insincere, but even insincere has some effect

**4. Contact & Cooperation (접촉과 협력)**
- Mere exposure effect: Familiarity breeds liking (not contempt)
- Cooperative interaction builds bonds faster than competitive
- Jigsaw classroom: Diverse students forced to cooperate → dramatic reduction in prejudice

**5. Association (연합)**
- Good news bearer = liked; bad news bearer = disliked (kill the messenger)
- Product + attractive person = product seems more attractive
- Weather forecasters blamed for bad weather
- We say "WE won" and "THEY lost"

### Application Tactics
- Find genuine common ground before business
- Cooperate on a small task before negotiating
- Deliver good news personally; buffer bad news with context
- Build familiarity through regular, positive contact
- Sincere compliments on specific behaviors (not generic flattery)

## Principle 6: Scarcity (희소성) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
Things seem more valuable when they're less available.

### Classic Research
- **Worchel cookie study (1975):** Same cookies. Jar of 2 rated tastier than jar of 10. Jar that went FROM 10 TO 2 rated tastiest of all.
- **Brehm reactance (1966):** Tell a 2-year-old they can't have a toy → they want it MORE. Same mechanism in adults, just subtler.
- **Romeo and Juliet effect:** Parental opposition increases romantic attraction.
- **Censorship effect:** Banned information is perceived as more valuable and believed more strongly.

### Two Amplifying Conditions

**1. Newly scarce is more powerful than always scarce**
- "We used to offer this but can't anymore" > "We never offered this"
- Losing something hits harder than never having it (loss aversion)
- Revolutions happen when conditions IMPROVE then WORSEN — not when they've always been bad

**2. Competition for scarce resources**
- Auction psychology: Scarcity + competitors = emotional frenzy
- Real estate: "Another buyer is interested" (powerful if true, unethical if false)
- Limited edition + visible demand = maximum urgency

### Application Tactics
- Genuine limits: "We can only serve 20 clients per quarter" (capacity-based)
- Real deadlines: "The regulatory window closes March 31" (external constraint)
- Exclusive access: "I'm sharing this with you before we announce publicly"
- Highlight uniqueness: What can they get from you that they can't get elsewhere?
- Frame as loss: "You'll lose X if you don't act" > "You'll gain X if you act"

## Principle 7: Unity (일체감) — Deep Dive

### The Rule
People say yes to those they consider "one of us" — shared IDENTITY, not just shared traits.

### Key Distinction: Liking vs Unity
- **Liking:** "We have things in common" (surface similarity)
- **Unity:** "We ARE the same" (shared identity, kinship, tribe)

### Research
- **Genetic relatedness:** People sacrifice more for kin, even when they don't know them well
- **Place-based identity:** Same hometown → dramatically more cooperation
- **Co-creation effect:** Ikea furniture valued more when self-assembled. Same with ideas, products, plans.
- **Synchrony:** Moving together (marching, dancing, singing) creates unity and cooperation

### Categories of Unity

| Type | Mechanism | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Kinship (혈연) | Family bonds | "You're like a brother to me" |
| Locality (지역) | Shared place | "We're both from Ulsan" |
| Profession (직업) | Shared vocation | "As engineers, we understand..." |
| Experience (경험) | Shared adversity/joy | "We survived that project together" |
| Co-creation (공동 창작) | Building together | "Let's design this solution together" |
| Ritual (의식) | Shared practices | Team traditions, ceremonies |

### Application Tactics
- Invite co-creation: "Help me shape this proposal" → they own it too
- Reference genuine shared identity: profession, location, alma mater, experience
- Create shared experiences: workshops, retreats, shared meals
- Use inclusive language: "we," "our," "us" (genuinely, not manipulatively)
- Acknowledge shared challenges: "We're all dealing with supply chain issues"

## Pre-Suasion Techniques (사전 설득)

### The Opener Matters

What you DO or SAY immediately BEFORE your message determines how it's received.

| Technique | How It Works | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Attention focus** | What gets attention gets importance | Ask "How adventurous are you?" → they think of times they were adventurous → more likely to try something new |
| **Priming** | Exposure to concept A makes concept B more accessible | Show images of collaboration → more cooperative in subsequent negotiation |
| **Anchoring** | First number sets the reference | "Some projects cost $10M" → your $2M proposal feels reasonable |
| **Environmental cues** | Physical surroundings affect mental state | Warm room → warm feelings; heavy clipboard → serious evaluation |
| **Question framing** | Questions direct attention selectively | "What do you LIKE about this?" vs "What do you DISLIKE?" → opposite conclusions from same data |

### The Privileged Moment

The moment of maximum influence is the moment just BEFORE you make your request. In that moment:
1. Direct attention to the concept that supports your ask
2. Create a mental state consistent with compliance
3. Remove distractions that compete for attention

## Ethical Application Matrix

For each principle, evaluate:

| Principle | Is it TRUE? | Does it BENEFIT them? | Would I be OK if they KNEW? | USE IT? |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Reciprocity | Is my gift/favor genuine? | Will they value what I give? | Would they feel manipulated? | |
| Commitment | Is the initial commitment authentic? | Does consistency serve them? | Am I trapping them? | |
| Social Proof | Are the numbers/testimonials real? | Is the crowd wisdom applicable? | Am I manufacturing consensus? | |
| Authority | Am I genuinely expert here? | Does my expertise serve them? | Am I overstating credentials? | |
| Liking | Is my rapport genuine? | Does our connection serve both? | Am I faking similarity? | |
| Scarcity | Is the limit real? | Does urgency help their decision? | Am I creating false pressure? | |
| Unity | Is our shared identity genuine? | Does belonging serve them? | Am I exploiting tribal instinct? | |

**Rule of thumb:** If you'd be embarrassed if your audience read Cialdini and recognized what you're doing, don't do it. If they'd APPRECIATE your approach even after reading the book, proceed with confidence.
