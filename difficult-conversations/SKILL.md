# Difficult Conversations (어려운 대화)

> "Difficult conversations are almost never about getting the facts right. They are about conflicting perceptions, interpretations, and values." — Douglas Stone

## The Three Conversations Model (세 가지 대화 모델)

Every difficult conversation is actually **three conversations** happening simultaneously. Most people only address the surface. Mastery requires navigating all three.

```
+--------------------------------------------------+
|           THE DIFFICULT CONVERSATION              |
|                                                   |
|  1. "What Happened?" Conversation (사실 대화)      |
|     Truth → Contribution                          |
|                                                   |
|  2. The Feelings Conversation (감정 대화)           |
|     Suppression → Acknowledgment                  |
|                                                   |
|  3. The Identity Conversation (정체성 대화)          |
|     All-or-Nothing → Complexity                   |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

---

## Conversation 1: "What Happened?" (무슨 일이 있었나?)

### The Trap: The Truth Assumption (진실 가정의 함정)

We assume: "I am right, they are wrong." Both sides believe this.

### The Shift: From Truth to Contribution (진실에서 기여로)

Instead of asking "Who's right?", ask "What did each of us **contribute** to this situation?"

| Old Frame (기존 관점) | New Frame (새 관점) |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| "You caused this problem" | "What did we each contribute?" |
| "I need to tell them they're wrong" | "I need to understand their story" |
| "There is one right answer" | "There are two valid stories" |

### Three Sub-shifts:

1. **Stop arguing about who's right** (옳고 그름 논쟁 중단) — Move from certainty to curiosity
2. **Don't assume intent** (의도를 가정하지 마라) — Impact ≠ Intent. They may have had good intentions and still caused harm.
3. **Map contributions, not blame** (비난이 아닌 기여를 파악) — Blame is backward-looking and shuts down learning. Contribution is forward-looking.

## Conversation 2: The Feelings Conversation (감정 대화)

### The Trap: Feelings Are Irrelevant (감정은 상관없다는 함정)

We think: "This is a work issue, feelings don't belong." But unexpressed feelings **leak** — as sarcasm, withdrawal, rigidity, or aggression.

### The Shift: Acknowledge Before Problem-Solving (문제 해결 전에 감정 인정)

**Feelings are not obstacles to the conversation — they ARE the conversation.**

Steps:
1. **Name your feelings to yourself** (자신의 감정 이름 붙이기) — "I feel unappreciated" is more useful than "I'm fine"
2. **Negotiate with your feelings** (감정과 협상) — Are they based on assumptions? Incomplete information?
3. **Share your feelings without blame** (비난 없이 감정 공유) — "I felt hurt" not "You hurt me"
4. **Acknowledge their feelings** (상대의 감정 인정) — Before you can be heard, they need to feel heard

### The Feelings Inventory (감정 목록)

Common hidden feelings in workplace conflicts:
- **Unappreciated** (인정받지 못함) — "My work doesn't matter to them"
- **Incompetent** (무능하다는 느낌) — "Maybe I'm not good enough"
- **Disrespected** (존중받지 못함) — "They don't value my perspective"
- **Afraid** (두려움) — "What if I lose my job/reputation?"
- **Guilty** (죄책감) — "I know I contributed to this but can't admit it"

## Conversation 3: The Identity Conversation (정체성 대화)

### The Trap: All-or-Nothing Thinking (전부 아니면 전무 사고)

Every difficult conversation threatens our self-image. Three core identity questions:

1. **Am I competent?** (나는 유능한가?) — Or am I actually incompetent?
2. **Am I a good person?** (나는 좋은 사람인가?) — Or am I actually selfish/unfair?
3. **Am I worthy of love/respect?** (나는 사랑/존중받을 자격이 있는가?) — Or will they reject me?

### The Shift: Embrace Complexity (복잡성을 받아들여라)

The truth is never all-or-nothing:
- "I am **both** competent AND I made a mistake"
- "I am **both** a caring manager AND I handled this poorly"
- "I am **both** valuable AND there's room to grow"

### Ground Your Identity Before the Conversation

Before entering the conversation:
1. Identify which identity issue is at stake for you
2. Accept that you are complex (both/and, not either/or)
3. Let go of controlling their reaction — you can't
4. Prepare for the worst-case identity threat and pre-accept it

## The Learning Conversation (학습 대화)

The goal of a difficult conversation is NOT to deliver a message. It IS to **learn**.

### The Third Story (제3의 이야기)

Start from the perspective of a neutral observer — someone who has no stake in the outcome.

| Your Story | Their Story | Third Story |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| "You missed the deadline and don't care" | "The requirements changed and nobody told me" | "There seems to be a gap between expectations and what was communicated about the timeline" |

The Third Story:
- Describes the **difference** between you, not who's right
- Uses neutral language
- Invites both perspectives
- Does NOT take sides

### Opening Lines Using the Third Story

**Bad openings**:
- "We need to talk about your performance." (accusation)
- "I'm sure this will be fine, but..." (minimizing)
- "Don't take this the wrong way..." (priming defensiveness)

**Good openings**:
- "I'd like to talk about [topic]. I think we see it differently, and I'd like to understand your perspective." (제3의 이야기 시작)
- "I've been thinking about [situation] and I realize I might be missing something. Can we talk?"
- "There's something that's been on my mind. I'm not sure I'm seeing it right, but I want to share it and hear your thoughts."

## Process: Preparing for a Difficult Conversation (준비 프로세스)

### Step 1: Sort Out the Three Conversations (세 대화 분리)

Write down:
- **What Happened**: What's my story? What might their story be? What did we each contribute?
- **Feelings**: What am I feeling? What might they be feeling?
- **Identity**: What's at stake for my self-image? What's at stake for theirs?

### Step 2: Decide Whether to Raise It (대화를 꺼낼지 결정)

Ask:
- Is this really about them, or is it about me? (내 문제인가?)
- Is there a simpler way to address this? (더 간단한 방법이 있는가?)
- What's my purpose? To change them, or to understand? (목적이 무엇인가?)

### Step 3: Start from the Third Story (제3의 이야기로 시작)

- Describe the gap or difference
- Invite their perspective first
- Share yours as a perspective, not the truth

### Step 4: Explore Their Story (상대의 이야기 탐구)

- Ask and listen (물어보고 들어라)
- Paraphrase to confirm understanding (이해한 내용을 되묻기)
- Acknowledge their feelings before asserting yours

### Step 5: Problem-Solve Together (함께 문제 해결)

Only after both stories are heard:
- Invent options that address both sides' concerns
- Agree on next steps
- Establish how to handle it if the issue recurs

## Anti-patterns (하지 말아야 할 것들)

1. **Leading with Your Conclusion** (결론부터 시작) — "You need to do X" skips the learning. Start with curiosity.

2. **Assuming Intent from Impact** (영향으로 의도를 가정) — "You did X, so you must have intended to hurt me." Impact and intent are independent.

3. **The Hit-and-Run** (말하고 도망가기) — Dropping the bomb and leaving. Difficult conversations require staying present.

4. **Binary Thinking About Identity** (정체성의 이분법적 사고) — "If I made a mistake, I'm a failure." Replace with "I made a mistake AND I'm still competent."

5. **Fixing Before Feeling** (감정 전에 해결) — Jumping to solutions before acknowledging emotions guarantees the conversation will repeat.

6. **The Blame Frame** (비난 프레임) — "Whose fault is it?" Replace with "What did we each contribute?"

7. **Rehearsing Your Monologue** (독백 리허설) — Preparing what to SAY instead of preparing what to ASK.

8. **Avoiding the Conversation Entirely** (대화 자체를 회피) — The cost of avoidance compounds. Unspoken issues don't resolve; they metastasize.
