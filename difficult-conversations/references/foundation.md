# Difficult Conversations — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creators

*Difficult Conversations: How to Discuss What Matters Most* (1999, updated 2010) was written by Douglas Stone, Bruce Patton, and Sheila Heen — all members of the Harvard Negotiation Project. The book emerged from 15 years of research at Harvard Law School's Program on Negotiation, applying negotiation principles to everyday personal and professional conflicts.

## The Three Conversations Model (세 가지 대화 모델)

Every difficult conversation is actually **three conversations** happening simultaneously. Most people only address the surface. Mastery requires navigating all three.

```
+--------------------------------------------------+
|           THE DIFFICULT CONVERSATION              |
|                                                   |
|  1. "What Happened?" Conversation (사실 대화)      |
|     Truth -> Contribution                         |
|                                                   |
|  2. The Feelings Conversation (감정 대화)           |
|     Suppression -> Acknowledgment                 |
|                                                   |
|  3. The Identity Conversation (정체성 대화)          |
|     All-or-Nothing -> Complexity                  |
+--------------------------------------------------+
```

## Conversation 1: "What Happened?" (무슨 일이 있었나?)

### The Trap: The Truth Assumption
We assume: "I am right, they are wrong." Both sides believe this.

### The Shift: From Truth to Contribution
Instead of asking "Who's right?", ask "What did each of us **contribute** to this situation?"

| Old Frame | New Frame |
|-----------|-----------|
| "You caused this problem" | "What did we each contribute?" |
| "I need to tell them they're wrong" | "I need to understand their story" |
| "There is one right answer" | "There are two valid stories" |

### Three Sub-shifts:
1. **Stop arguing about who's right** — Move from certainty to curiosity
2. **Don't assume intent** — Impact does not equal Intent. Good intentions can still cause harm.
3. **Map contributions, not blame** — Blame is backward-looking and shuts down learning. Contribution is forward-looking.

## Conversation 2: The Feelings Conversation (감정 대화)

### The Trap
We think: "This is a work issue, feelings don't belong." But unexpressed feelings **leak** — as sarcasm, withdrawal, rigidity, or aggression.

### The Shift
**Feelings are not obstacles to the conversation — they ARE the conversation.**

Steps:
1. Name your feelings to yourself
2. Negotiate with your feelings — are they based on assumptions?
3. Share your feelings without blame — "I felt hurt" not "You hurt me"
4. Acknowledge their feelings before asserting yours

### Common Hidden Feelings in Workplace Conflicts
- **Unappreciated** — "My work doesn't matter to them"
- **Incompetent** — "Maybe I'm not good enough"
- **Disrespected** — "They don't value my perspective"
- **Afraid** — "What if I lose my job/reputation?"
- **Guilty** — "I know I contributed but can't admit it"

## Conversation 3: The Identity Conversation (정체성 대화)

### The Trap: All-or-Nothing Thinking
Every difficult conversation threatens our self-image. Three core identity questions:
1. **Am I competent?** Or am I actually incompetent?
2. **Am I a good person?** Or am I actually selfish/unfair?
3. **Am I worthy of love/respect?** Or will they reject me?

### The Shift: Embrace Complexity
- "I am **both** competent AND I made a mistake"
- "I am **both** a caring manager AND I handled this poorly"
- "I am **both** valuable AND there's room to grow"

## The Third Story (제3의 이야기)

Start from the perspective of a neutral observer — someone who has no stake in the outcome.

| Your Story | Their Story | Third Story |
|------------|-------------|-------------|
| "You missed the deadline and don't care" | "The requirements changed and nobody told me" | "There seems to be a gap between expectations and what was communicated about the timeline" |

The Third Story:
- Describes the **difference** between you, not who's right
- Uses neutral language
- Invites both perspectives
- Does NOT take sides

## Safety Warning: When NOT to Use This Framework

This framework assumes a **roughly equal power dynamic**. Do NOT use in:
- **Domestic violence or abuse** — "finding your contribution" can blame the victim
- **Harassment situations** — the perpetrator is responsible, not the target
- **Active addiction/crisis** — professional help needed first
- If you feel physically unsafe, contact a professional:
  - Korea: 1393 (자살예방) / 1366 (여성긴급전화) / 112 (경찰)
  - US: 988 (Suicide & Crisis) / 1-800-799-7233 (DV Hotline)
