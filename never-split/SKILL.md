# Never Split the Difference — FBI Negotiation Skill

> "He who has learned to disagree without being disagreeable has discovered the most valuable secret of negotiation." — Chris Voss

## Overview

Chris Voss, former FBI lead international kidnapping negotiator, distills decades of high-stakes negotiation into practical tactics. The core insight: negotiation is not about logic — it's about emotions (감정). The person who best understands and influences emotions wins. These tools work in business deals, salary negotiations, buying a car, and everyday conversations.

## Core Principles

### 1. Tactical Empathy (전술적 공감)

Understanding the feelings and mindset of the counterpart AND demonstrating that understanding. Not agreeing — understanding.

**It's NOT:**
- Sympathy (feeling sorry for them)
- Agreement (accepting their position)
- Weakness (being a pushover)

**It IS:**
- Demonstrating you understand their world
- Making them feel heard
- Building enough trust that they lower their guard

**How to practice:**
1. Imagine yourself in their position
2. Identify their constraints (boss pressure, budget limits, time pressure)
3. Articulate their perspective better than they can themselves
4. Vocalize it back to them

## The 9 Core Tactics

### Tactic 1: Mirroring (미러링 — 따라하기)

Repeat the last 1-3 critical words (or key phrase) the other person said, with an upward inflection.

**How it works:**
- Triggers a deep instinct to elaborate and connect
- Buys you time to think
- Gets the other person to reveal more information

**Example:**
> Them: "We can't do that because of budget constraints."
> You: "Budget constraints?"
> Them: "Yeah, we allocated 80% already and the CFO froze new spending..."

**Rules:**
1. Use a calm, curious tone (not mocking or aggressive)
2. Follow the mirror with silence — let it work
3. Repeat 1-3 words, not full sentences
4. Works especially well when you don't understand something

### Tactic 2: Labeling (라벨링 — 감정에 이름 붙이기)

Name the emotion you detect in the other person.

**Formulas:**
- "It seems like..." (~ 인 것 같네요)
- "It sounds like..." (~ 처럼 들리네요)
- "It looks like..." (~ 처럼 보이네요)
- "It feels like..." (~ 느낌이 드네요)

**NEVER say "I" — it makes it about you:**
- BAD: "**I** think you're frustrated"
- GOOD: "**It seems like** you're frustrated"

**After labeling:** SILENCE. Let them confirm, deny, or elaborate. Do not rescue the silence.

**Labeling negative emotions diminishes them. Labeling positive emotions reinforces them.**

**Examples:**
- "It seems like you're worried about losing control of the timeline."
- "It sounds like there's been a lot of pressure from above on this."
- "It looks like fairness is really important to you here."

### Tactic 3: The Accusation Audit (비난 감사 / 선제적 부정 인정)

List every terrible thing the counterpart could think about you — BEFORE they say it.

**Why it works:**
- Defuses negative emotions preemptively
- Makes accusations sound exaggerated when spoken aloud
- The other person often says "It's not that bad" and softens

**Template:**
"You're probably going to think I'm [terrible thing]. You might feel like [negative emotion]. This is going to sound [bad adjective]..."

**Example (asking for a raise):**
"You're probably going to think I'm being greedy. You might feel like I'm not appreciating what the company has already done for me. This might seem like terrible timing given the quarterly results..."

Then state your request. The sting has already been pulled.

### Tactic 4: "No" is the Start, Not the End ("아니오"는 시작이다)

Stop trying to get to "Yes." Aim for "No" first.

**Why "No" is powerful:**
- "No" gives people a feeling of safety and control
- "Yes" can be counterfeit (commitment yes vs. confirmation yes vs. counterfeit yes)
- After saying "No," people are more open to listen

**Three types of "Yes":**
1. **Counterfeit (거짓):** Said to get rid of you — "Yeah, sure, fine"
2. **Confirmation (확인):** Simple factual agreement — "Yes, that's correct"
3. **Commitment (진짜):** Real agreement to act — "Yes, let's do it"

**How to use "No":**
- Mislabel an emotion on purpose: "It seems like you want this project to fail?" → "No! I just..."
- Ask a no-oriented question: "Is it a bad idea to...?" / "Have you given up on this?"
- Email magic: "Have you given up on this project?" (gets responses from ghosts)

### Tactic 5: "That's Right" (바로 그거예요!)

The two most powerful words in a negotiation. NOT "You're right" — "THAT'S right."

**"You're right" (당신 말이 맞아요):** A polite dismissal. They use it to make you shut up.
**"That's right" (바로 그거예요):** A genuine breakthrough. They feel deeply understood.

**How to get to "That's right":**
1. Listen actively
2. Summarize their world: paraphrase (meaning) + label (emotion)
3. When your summary is so accurate they can only say "That's right" — you've broken through

**Formula:** Paraphrase + Label = "That's right"
> "So you've been working weekends for three months [paraphrase] and it feels like nobody even notices [label]."
> → "THAT'S right."

### Tactic 6: Calibrated Questions (보정 질문)

Open-ended questions that start with "How" or "What" — they give the other side the illusion of control.

**Power questions:**
- "How am I supposed to do that?" (가장 강력한 질문)
- "What about this is important to you?"
- "How can we solve this problem?"
- "What are we trying to accomplish here?"
- "How does this affect the rest of your team?"
- "What happens if we do nothing?"
- "What does success look like to you?"

**AVOID "Why" — it sounds accusatory:**
- BAD: "Why did you do that?"
- GOOD: "What led you to that decision?"

**"How am I supposed to do that?"** — The single most important question. It says no without saying no. It makes YOUR problem THEIR problem.

### Tactic 7: The Rule of Three (3의 법칙)

Get the other person to agree to the same thing THREE times in the same conversation. Liars and fakers struggle to maintain consistency across three agreements.

**How to apply:**
1. First agreement: The initial "yes" or "that's right"
2. Second agreement: Summarize and label — "So you're saying...?"
3. Third agreement: A calibrated question about implementation — "How will we know we're on track?"

If their tone/body language shifts across the three, something is off.

### Tactic 8: The 7-38-55 Rule (커뮤니케이션 비율)

- **7%** of a message is the words
- **38%** is the tone of voice
- **55%** is body language and facial expressions

**Implication:** Pay MORE attention to how they say things than what they say. Incongruence between words and tone = deception or discomfort.

### Tactic 9: Black Swans (블랙스완)

Unknown unknowns — pieces of information that, if discovered, would change everything.

**Three types of leverage:**
1. **Positive leverage (긍정적 레버리지):** You have something they want
2. **Negative leverage (부정적 레버리지):** You can cause them pain
3. **Normative leverage (규범적 레버리지):** Using their own standards/beliefs against them

**How to find Black Swans:**
- Face-to-face time (not email)
- Ask "What" and "How" questions relentlessly
- Pay attention to moments of confusion or surprise
- Listen for religious/ideological/moral references
- Review everything said in the first few minutes (people drop clues early)

## Ackerman Bargaining Model (에커먼 흥정 모델)

A systematic approach to price/salary negotiation:

| Round | Offer | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **65%** of target price | Anchor aggressively (with rationale) |
| 2 | **85%** of target | Show movement, reluctance |
| 3 | **95%** of target | Decreasing increments signal you're near your limit |
| 4 | **100%** of target | Use a precise, non-round number (e.g., 97,350 not 97,000) + throw in a non-monetary item |

**Key principles:**
- Each increment gets SMALLER (shows you're squeezing, approaching your limit)
- Use empathy between rounds — label, mirror, calibrate
- Express that each concession is PAINFUL for you
- Use odd/precise numbers (they feel more researched and firm)
- At the final number, include a non-monetary item to signal you're truly at your limit

**Example (buying equipment, target: 50M KRW):**
1. Open: 32.5M ("Based on comparable equipment prices...")
2. Second: 42.5M (reluctantly, after labeling their reaction)
3. Third: 47.5M ("I'm really stretching here...")
4. Final: 50M + ask them to include delivery/training/warranty

## Anti-patterns

1. **Compromise / Splitting the difference** — "Let's meet in the middle" is lazy, not fair. No deal is better than a bad deal.
2. **Trying to get to "Yes" fast** — Quick yeses are usually counterfeit. Slow down.
3. **Using "I" in labels** — "I think you feel..." makes it about you. Use "It seems like..."
4. **Asking "Why"** — Sounds like an interrogation. Use "What" and "How" instead.
5. **Filling silence** — After a mirror or label, WAIT. The other person needs time to process.
6. **Treating negotiation as a battle** — It's a discovery process. You're looking for information, not trying to beat them.
7. **Round numbers in offers** — $50,000 feels arbitrary. $48,750 feels researched.
8. **Ignoring incongruence** — Words say "yes" but tone says "no" → investigate.
9. **Revealing your deadline** — Time pressure is leverage. Don't hand it over.
10. **Accepting "You're right"** — It means they want you to stop talking. Push for "That's right."
