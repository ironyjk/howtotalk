# Never Split the Difference — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creator

Chris Voss is a former FBI lead international kidnapping negotiator who spent 24 years at the Bureau, including roles as the lead crisis negotiator. His book *Never Split the Difference* (2016) distills high-stakes hostage negotiation tactics into everyday business and personal use. The core insight: negotiation is not about logic — it's about emotions (감정). The person who best understands and influences emotions wins.

## Core Principle: Tactical Empathy (전술적 공감)

Understanding the feelings and mindset of the counterpart AND demonstrating that understanding. Not agreeing — understanding.

**It's NOT:**
- Sympathy (feeling sorry for them)
- Agreement (accepting their position)
- Weakness (being a pushover)

**It IS:**
- Demonstrating you understand their world
- Making them feel heard
- Building enough trust that they lower their guard

**How to practice:**
1. Imagine yourself in their position
2. Identify their constraints (boss pressure, budget limits, time pressure)
3. Articulate their perspective better than they can themselves
4. Vocalize it back to them

## The 9 Core Tactics — Theory

### Tactic 1: Mirroring (미러링)
Repeat the last 1-3 critical words with an upward inflection. Triggers a deep instinct to elaborate and connect. Buys time and gets information.

### Tactic 2: Labeling (라벨링)
Name the detected emotion. Formulas: "It seems like..." / "It sounds like..." / "It looks like..." NEVER say "I" — it makes it about you. After labeling: SILENCE. Labeling negative emotions diminishes them; labeling positive emotions reinforces them.

### Tactic 3: The Accusation Audit (비난 감사)
List every terrible thing the counterpart could think about you — BEFORE they say it. Defuses negative emotions preemptively. The other person often says "It's not that bad" and softens.

### Tactic 4: "No" is the Start ("아니오"는 시작이다)
Stop trying to get to "Yes." "No" gives people safety and control. Three types of "Yes": Counterfeit (said to get rid of you), Confirmation (factual), Commitment (real).

### Tactic 5: "That's Right" (바로 그거예요!)
The two most powerful words. NOT "You're right" (a polite dismissal) — "THAT'S right" (genuine breakthrough). Get there via: Paraphrase + Label = "That's right."

### Tactic 6: Calibrated Questions (보정 질문)
Open-ended "How" or "What" questions that give the illusion of control. "How am I supposed to do that?" is the most important — it says no without saying no and makes YOUR problem THEIR problem. AVOID "Why" — it sounds accusatory.

### Tactic 7: The Rule of Three (3의 법칙)
Get agreement to the same thing THREE times. Liars struggle to maintain consistency across three agreements.

### Tactic 8: The 7-38-55 Rule (커뮤니케이션 비율)
7% words, 38% tone, 55% body language. Pay MORE attention to how they say things than what they say.

### Tactic 9: Black Swans (블랙스완)
Unknown unknowns that would change everything. Three types of leverage: Positive (you have what they want), Negative (you can cause pain), Normative (using their own standards against them).

## Ackerman Bargaining Model (에커먼 흥정 모델)

A systematic approach to price/salary negotiation:

| Round | Offer | Notes |
|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | **65%** of target price | Anchor aggressively (with rationale) |
| 2 | **85%** of target | Show movement, reluctance |
| 3 | **95%** of target | Decreasing increments signal you're near limit |
| 4 | **100%** of target | Precise non-round number + non-monetary item |

**Key principles:**
- Each increment gets SMALLER
- Use empathy between rounds
- Express that each concession is PAINFUL
- Use odd/precise numbers (they feel more researched and firm)
- At the final number, include a non-monetary item to signal you're truly at your limit
