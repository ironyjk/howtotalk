# Storytelling Frameworks — Detailed Reference (스토리텔링 프레임워크 상세)

## Hero's Journey: Full 12 Stages with Examples

### Act I: Departure (출발)

**1. Ordinary World (일상 세계)**
The hero's normal life before the adventure. Establish empathy.
- *Star Wars*: Luke on the moisture farm
- *Business*: "Before we adopted cloud, deployments took 3 weeks..."

**2. Call to Adventure (모험으로의 부름)**
Something disrupts the ordinary. A problem, opportunity, or message.
- *Star Wars*: R2-D2's holographic message
- *Business*: "Then our biggest competitor launched in half our time..."

**3. Refusal of the Call (부름의 거부)**
Hesitation. Fear. The comfort of the known.
- *Star Wars*: "I can't get involved. I have work to do."
- *Business*: "Our board said the risk was too high..."

**4. Meeting the Mentor (멘토와의 만남)**
A guide appears with wisdom, tools, or confidence.
- *Star Wars*: Obi-Wan Kenobi
- *Business*: "Our new CTO had seen this transformation at three other companies..."

**5. Crossing the Threshold (문턱 넘기)**
The hero commits. No going back.
- *Star Wars*: Leaving Tatooine
- *Business*: "We signed the contract and burned the old servers..."

### Act II: Initiation (시련)

**6. Tests, Allies, Enemies (시련, 동맹, 적)**
The new world has rules to learn. Friends and foes emerge.
- *Business*: "The migration broke 47 integrations. But our DevOps team rallied..."

**7. Approach to the Inmost Cave (가장 깊은 동굴 접근)**
Preparation for the supreme ordeal.
- *Business*: "The board review was in 48 hours and we were still 30% behind..."

**8. The Ordeal (시련/최고의 시험)**
The crisis. Death and rebirth. The darkest moment.
- *Business*: "The system went down for 8 hours on Black Friday..."

**9. Reward (보상)**
The hero seizes the prize after surviving the ordeal.
- *Business*: "When it came back up, order processing was 10x faster..."

### Act III: Return (귀환)

**10. The Road Back (귀환의 길)**
The journey home. New challenges in the old world.
- *Business*: "Now we had to train 200 people on the new system..."

**11. Resurrection (부활)**
Final test. The hero is transformed.
- *Business*: "When the next crisis hit, we recovered in minutes, not days..."

**12. Return with the Elixir (영약과 함께 귀환)**
The hero brings back something that benefits the ordinary world.
- *Business*: "Today, we deploy 50 times a day, and our methodology is now the company standard..."

## STAR: Advanced Techniques

### The STAR-L Variant (with Learning)

Add an explicit **L**earning step:
- **S**ituation → **T**ask → **A**ction → **R**esult → **L**earning
- "The learning I took from this that I still apply is..."

### Quantifying Results

Weak: "We improved performance."
Strong: "Response time dropped from 3.2s to 0.4s, which increased conversion by 23% — $1.2M in annual revenue."

### The "So What?" Test

After drafting your STAR story, ask: "So what?" If you can't answer clearly, sharpen the Result.

## PAS: Agitation Depth Levels

### Level 1: Surface Pain (표면적 고통)
> "Manual data entry is tedious."

### Level 2: Consequences (결과)
> "Manual data entry causes 12% error rates, which means 1 in 8 invoices has mistakes."

### Level 3: Ripple Effects (파급 효과)
> "Those errors take 3 hours each to fix, your best people are wasting time on corrections instead of strategic work, and customers are losing trust."

### Level 4: Identity Threat (정체성 위협)
> "Your team joined to do meaningful analytical work. Instead, they're checking spreadsheets. Your best people are updating their resumes."

### Level 5: Future Catastrophe (미래 재앙)
> "In 18 months, your competitor — who automated this last year — will be two product generations ahead of you."

**Rule**: Agitate to Level 3-4 for most business contexts. Level 5 only when the threat is genuinely existential.

## Pixar Story Spine: Variations

### The Reverse Spine (역순 스파인)
Start from the ending and work backwards. Useful for case studies.

> "Today, Company X processes 10M transactions/day with 99.99% uptime."
> "But it wasn't always this way."
> "Once upon a time, their system crashed every Black Friday..."

### The Compressed Spine (압축 스파인)
For elevator pitches (30-60 seconds):

> "Every day, [audience] struggles with [problem]. One day, [your solution] appeared. Because of that, [benefit]. And ever since, [new reality]."

## Sparkline: Presentation Architecture

### Beat Structure for a 20-Minute Talk

| Time | Beat | Content |
|------|------|---------|
| 0:00-2:00 | What Is | Open with a vivid depiction of the current problem |
| 2:00-5:00 | What Could Be | Paint the alternative future |
| 5:00-8:00 | What Is (deeper) | Show the root cause / hidden costs |
| 8:00-12:00 | What Could Be (expanded) | Present the solution in detail |
| 12:00-15:00 | What Is (personal) | Tell a specific story of someone affected |
| 15:00-18:00 | What Could Be (proven) | Show evidence, results, social proof |
| 18:00-20:00 | New Bliss | Call to action — the world after transformation |

### The STAR Moment (Something They'll Always Remember)

Every presentation needs one moment that etches into memory:
- **A dramatic demo** (Steve Jobs pulling the MacBook Air from an envelope)
- **A shocking statistic** (Al Gore on the cherry picker with the CO2 graph)
- **An emotional story** (a specific person whose life changed)
- **A physical prop** (Bill Gates releasing mosquitoes: "Malaria is transmitted by mosquitoes. I brought some.")

Place it at the 60-70% mark of your talk for maximum impact.

## Combining Frameworks (프레임워크 결합)

### PAS + STAR = Case Study
1. **Problem**: The client's pain
2. **Agitate**: What it was costing them
3. **Solution**: Your offering (brief)
4. **STAR**: Detailed story of implementation and results

### Sparkline + Hero's Journey = Keynote
- Use Sparkline structure for the overall presentation rhythm
- Embed Hero's Journey stories at each "What Could Be" beat
- The audience is the hero; you narrate their potential journey

### Story Spine + STAR = Team Update
- Story Spine for the narrative arc (quick, engaging)
- STAR for the key achievement within the narrative

- Robert McKee, *Story* (1997) — the definitive work on narrative structure
- Chip & Dan Heath, *Made to Stick* (2007) — why some stories stick and others don't
- Kindra Hall, *Stories That Stick* (2019) — business storytelling specifically
- Matthew Dicks, *Storyworthy* (2018) — finding stories in everyday life
- Kurt Vonnegut, "Shape of Stories" (lecture) — the emotional arc of narratives
