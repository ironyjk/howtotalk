# Storytelling Frameworks — Theory & Background

## Why Storytelling Matters (왜 스토리텔링인가)

- Facts inform; stories **transform** (사실은 알리고, 이야기는 변화시킨다)
- People retain 65-70% of information shared through stories vs 5-10% of statistics
- Stories activate the brain's motor cortex, sensory cortex, and frontal cortex — facts activate only Broca's and Wernicke's areas
- Every great leader, salesperson, and teacher is a storyteller

## Framework 1: The Hero's Journey (영웅의 여정)

### Origin
Joseph Campbell's *The Hero with a Thousand Faces* (1949), refined by Christopher Vogler for Hollywood in *The Writer's Journey* (1992).

### The 12 Stages

```
    ORDINARY WORLD                    SPECIAL WORLD
         |                                 |
    1. Ordinary World             7. Approach to Inmost Cave
    2. Call to Adventure          8. Ordeal (Supreme Test)
    3. Refusal of the Call        9. Reward (Seizing the Sword)
    4. Meeting the Mentor        10. The Road Back
    5. Crossing the Threshold    11. Resurrection
    6. Tests, Allies, Enemies    12. Return with the Elixir
```

| # | Stage | Korean | Business Application |
|---|-------|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | Ordinary World | 일상 세계 | Current state of the customer/company |
| 2 | Call to Adventure | 모험으로의 부름 | The problem or opportunity appears |
| 3 | Refusal of the Call | 부름의 거부 | Hesitation, risk aversion, status quo bias |
| 4 | Meeting the Mentor | 멘토와의 만남 | Your product/service/advisor enters |
| 5 | Crossing the Threshold | 문턱 넘기 | Decision to act, commitment |
| 6 | Tests, Allies, Enemies | 시련, 동맹, 적 | Implementation challenges, partners |
| 7 | Approach to Inmost Cave | 가장 깊은 동굴 접근 | Preparing for the biggest challenge |
| 8 | Ordeal | 시련 (최고의 시험) | The crisis moment, the pivot |
| 9 | Reward | 보상 (칼 쥐기) | First results, validation |
| 10 | The Road Back | 귀환의 길 | Scaling, sustaining the change |
| 11 | Resurrection | 부활 | Final test that proves transformation |
| 12 | Return with Elixir | 영약과 함께 귀환 | New normal, lessons to share |

### Business Shortcut: 5-Stage Hero's Journey

For presentations and pitches, condense to 5 stages:
1. **Status Quo** — Here's how things are
2. **Disruption** — Something changes/threatens
3. **Quest** — The journey to solve it
4. **Transformation** — What was learned/built
5. **New Normal** — The better future

## Framework 2: STAR Method (STAR 기법)

### Origin
Developed for behavioral interview questions. Now used broadly for structured storytelling.

### Structure

| Element | Korean | Description | Time |
|---------|--------|-------------|------|
| **S**ituation | 상황 | Set the context | 10-15% |
| **T**ask | 과제 | What was your responsibility/goal | 10-15% |
| **A**ction | 행동 | What specifically you did (the "I", not "we") | 50-60% |
| **R**esult | 결과 | Quantifiable outcome + what you learned | 20-25% |

### STAR+ Enhancement

Add these to make STAR stories memorable:
- **Stakes**: What would have happened if you failed? (위험)
- **Tension**: What moment was most uncertain? (긴장)
- **Insight**: What did you learn that you still use today? (통찰)

### Example Structure

> **S**: "In Q3 2024, our largest client threatened to leave after a failed deployment."
> **T**: "As the account lead, I had 2 weeks to prevent a $2M annual revenue loss."
> **A**: "I flew to their office, conducted root-cause analysis with their engineering team, rebuilt the deployment pipeline myself over a weekend, and created a 30-day stabilization plan."
> **R**: "Not only did they stay, but they expanded the contract by 40%. I learned that showing up in person during a crisis builds more trust than any slide deck."

## Framework 3: PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)

### Origin
Classic direct-response copywriting framework. Used in sales, marketing, and persuasive presentations.

### Structure

| Element | Korean | Purpose | Emotion |
|---------|--------|---------|---------|
| **P**roblem | 문제 | Name their pain | Recognition: "Yes, that's me" |
| **A**gitate | 자극/심화 | Make the pain vivid and urgent | Anxiety: "This is worse than I thought" |
| **S**olution | 해결 | Present your answer | Relief: "There's a way out" |

### Agitation Techniques

1. **Consequences** (결과): "If this continues for 6 more months..."
2. **Comparison** (비교): "Your competitors have already solved this..."
3. **Hidden costs** (숨겨진 비용): "The real cost isn't the tool — it's the 4 hours/week your team wastes on..."
4. **Emotional cost** (감정적 비용): "Every Sunday night, that pit in your stomach..."
5. **Social proof of pain** (고통의 사회적 증거): "73% of teams report this exact frustration..."

### PAS vs PASO
Extended version adds **Outcome** (결과): concrete vision of life after the solution.

## Framework 4: Pixar Story Spine (픽사 스토리 스파인)

### Origin
Created by Kenn Adams for improvisational theater. Adopted by Pixar as a story development tool.

### The Spine

```
Once upon a time, there was ___.          (옛날에 ___가 있었다)
Every day, ___.                           (매일 ___했다)
One day, ___.                             (어느 날 ___가 일어났다)
Because of that, ___.                     (그 때문에 ___)
Because of that, ___.                     (그 때문에 ___)
Because of that, ___.                     (그 때문에 ___)
Until finally, ___.                       (마침내 ___)
And ever since then, ___.                 (그 이후로 ___)
The moral is: ___.                        (교훈은 ___)
```

### Business Example

> Once upon a time, there was a **factory that ran maintenance by gut feeling**.
> Every day, **machines broke unexpectedly, costing $50K in downtime**.
> One day, **they installed IoT sensors on every critical asset**.
> Because of that, **they could predict failures 72 hours ahead**.
> Because of that, **maintenance became proactive instead of reactive**.
> Because of that, **downtime dropped 80% in 6 months**.
> Until finally, **they became the benchmark plant in the company**.
> And ever since then, **every new factory starts with predictive maintenance**.
> The moral is: **The most expensive maintenance is the kind you didn't plan for.**

### Why It Works
- **"Once upon a time"** establishes normalcy — the audience relaxes
- **"Every day"** creates rhythm and expectations
- **"One day"** breaks the pattern — attention spikes
- **"Because of that" (x3)** creates causality — the brain craves cause-and-effect
- **"Until finally"** delivers resolution — satisfaction

## Framework 5: Nancy Duarte's Sparkline (낸시 두아르떼의 스파크라인)

### Origin
Nancy Duarte's *Resonate* (2010). Analyzed hundreds of great speeches (MLK, Steve Jobs) and found a common structure.

### The Pattern: What Is vs What Could Be (현재 vs 가능한 미래)

```
  What Could Be -----+    +----+    +---- NEW BLISS
                      |    |    |    |
                      +----+    +----+
  ----------------------------------------
  What Is ------------+    +----+
                      |    |    |
                      +----+    +---------
  <- Beginning                    End ->
```

The speaker oscillates between:
- **What Is** (현재 상태): The current reality, the problem, the pain
- **What Could Be** (가능한 미래): The vision, the possibility, the promise

### Structure
1. **What Is** — Start with the audience's current reality
2. **What Could Be** — Paint the alternative
3. **What Is** — Back to reality (the gap stings more now)
4. **What Could Be** — Expand the vision further
5. **What Is** — Final contrast
6. **New Bliss** — End with the transformed future (the call to action)

### Steve Jobs iPhone Launch (2007) — Sparkline

| Beat | What Is / What Could Be |
|------|------------------------|
| 1 (What Is) | "Smartphones are not so smart. Hard to use." |
| 2 (Could Be) | "What if we made a phone that was truly smart?" |
| 3 (What Is) | "Stylus? Who wants a stylus? You lose them." |
| 4 (Could Be) | "We're going to use the best pointing device — your finger." |
| 5 (What Is) | "Current phones have fixed keyboards. Can't innovate." |
| 6 (Could Be) | "Multi-touch display. Every app gets the interface it needs." |
| Final | "iPhone. An iPod, a phone, and an internet communicator. Are you getting it?" |

### Key Principles
- **Contrast creates energy** — Without "What Is", "What Could Be" has no weight
- **End on "What Could Be"** — Always leave the audience in the aspirational state
- **The audience is the hero** — You are the mentor
- **STAR moment** — Every great presentation needs one "Something They'll Always Remember" moment

## Framework 6: 기승전결 (起承轉結) — Korean/East Asian Narrative

| Stage | 한자 | Meaning | Function |
|-------|------|---------|----------|
| 기(起) | Rise | Introduction | Set the scene, present the topic |
| 승(承) | Continue | Development | Build on the introduction, add details |
| 전(轉) | Turn | Twist/Pivot | Unexpected turn, new perspective, the "but..." |
| 결(結) | Conclude | Resolution | Wrap up, lesson learned, call to action |

When to use: Korean business presentations, writing for Korean audiences, any situation where the audience expects gradual build-up before the point.

## Framework 7: 두괄식 (頭括式) — Conclusion-First

Korean business standard: State the conclusion/recommendation FIRST, then support with evidence.
- Opposite of Hero's Journey (which builds to climax)
- Used in: Korean corporate reports, military briefings, executive summaries
- Structure: Bottom Line -> Supporting Point 1 -> Supporting Point 2 -> Restatement
