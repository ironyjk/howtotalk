# Socratic Method (소크라테스식 문답법)

> "I cannot teach anybody anything. I can only make them think." — Socrates

> "The unexamined life is not worth living." — Socrates, *Apology*

## Purpose (목적)

The Socratic Method is a form of cooperative argumentative dialogue that stimulates critical thinking and draws out ideas and underlying presuppositions. It does NOT deliver answers — it guides people to discover their own.

### Core Principles

1. **Questions over statements** (진술보다 질문) — The teacher/leader asks; the learner discovers
2. **Productive discomfort** (생산적 불편함) — Confusion is not failure; it's the precondition for insight
3. **Elenchus (논박)** — Expose contradictions in someone's beliefs so they can refine them
4. **Intellectual humility** (지적 겸손) — Socrates' "I know that I know nothing" (나는 내가 모른다는 것을 안다)
5. **Maieutics (산파술)** — Like a midwife helping deliver a baby, the questioner helps deliver ideas already present in the thinker

## The Six Types of Socratic Questions (소크라테스식 질문의 6가지 유형)

### Type 1: Clarifying Questions (명확화 질문)

**Purpose**: Understand what they actually mean. Surface hidden assumptions in their language.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "What do you mean by ___?" | "___이라고 하면 무슨 뜻인가요?" |
| "Can you put that another way?" | "다른 방식으로 설명해주시겠어요?" |
| "Can you give me an example?" | "예를 들어주시겠어요?" |
| "How does this relate to what we're discussing?" | "이것이 우리가 논의하는 것과 어떤 관련이 있나요?" |
| "What is the central issue here?" | "여기서 핵심 문제가 무엇인가요?" |
| "Could you explain that further?" | "좀 더 자세히 설명해주시겠어요?" |

**When to use**: At the beginning of any inquiry, or when someone makes a broad claim.

### Type 2: Probing Assumptions (가정 탐구 질문)

**Purpose**: Uncover the unquestioned beliefs beneath the argument.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "What are you assuming here?" | "여기서 어떤 가정을 하고 계신 건가요?" |
| "Is that always the case?" | "항상 그런 건가요?" |
| "What would happen if that assumption were wrong?" | "그 가정이 틀렸다면 어떻게 되나요?" |
| "Why would someone make this assumption?" | "왜 이런 가정을 하게 되었을까요?" |
| "What else could we assume instead?" | "대신 어떤 다른 가정을 할 수 있을까요?" |

**When to use**: When someone presents an argument built on hidden foundations.

### Type 3: Probing Evidence and Reasoning (증거/논리 탐구 질문)

**Purpose**: Examine the quality of evidence and the logic chain.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "How do you know this?" | "이것을 어떻게 아시나요?" |
| "What evidence supports this?" | "이것을 뒷받침하는 증거는 무엇인가요?" |
| "Is there reason to doubt that evidence?" | "그 증거를 의심할 이유가 있나요?" |
| "What would change your mind?" | "무엇이 생각을 바꾸게 할까요?" |
| "Is this sufficient evidence, or do we need more?" | "이 정도 증거로 충분한가요, 아니면 더 필요한가요?" |
| "How did you arrive at that conclusion?" | "그 결론에 어떻게 도달했나요?" |

**When to use**: When claims are stated as facts, or when reasoning has logical gaps.

### Type 4: Questioning Viewpoints and Perspectives (관점/시각 질문)

**Purpose**: Introduce alternative perspectives. Break tunnel vision.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "What would someone who disagrees say?" | "반대하는 사람은 뭐라고 말할까요?" |
| "How might [group X] see this differently?" | "[X 그룹]은 이것을 어떻게 다르게 볼까요?" |
| "What's the strongest argument against your position?" | "당신의 입장에 대한 가장 강력한 반론은 무엇인가요?" |
| "What alternative explanations exist?" | "어떤 대안적 설명이 있을까요?" |
| "How is this similar to ___? How is it different?" | "이것은 ___과 어떻게 비슷하고 어떻게 다른가요?" |

**When to use**: When groupthink is present, or when the discussion has become one-sided.

### Type 5: Probing Implications and Consequences (함축/결과 질문)

**Purpose**: Follow the logic to its endpoint. Reveal unintended consequences.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "If that's true, what else must be true?" | "그것이 사실이라면, 또 무엇이 사실이어야 하나요?" |
| "What would be the consequences of that?" | "그것의 결과는 무엇이 될까요?" |
| "How does this affect ___?" | "이것이 ___에 어떤 영향을 미치나요?" |
| "What are the long-term implications?" | "장기적인 함의는 무엇인가요?" |
| "If we accept this, what must we also accept?" | "이것을 받아들이면, 무엇도 함께 받아들여야 하나요?" |

**When to use**: When a decision is being made, or when someone hasn't thought through the downstream effects.

### Type 6: Questions About the Question (질문에 대한 질문)

**Purpose**: Step back from the content to examine the inquiry itself. Meta-cognition.

| Question | Korean |
|----------|--------|
| "Why is this question important?" | "이 질문이 왜 중요한가요?" |
| "Is this the right question to be asking?" | "이것이 지금 물어야 할 올바른 질문인가요?" |
| "What was the point of asking that?" | "그것을 물어본 의도가 무엇이었나요?" |
| "How would [expert] frame this question?" | "[전문가]라면 이 질문을 어떻게 구성할까요?" |
| "What other questions do we need to answer first?" | "먼저 답해야 할 다른 질문은 무엇인가요?" |

**When to use**: When the conversation is going in circles, or when the framing itself is the problem.

## The Socratic Seminar Format (소크라테스 세미나 형식)

### Setup

A structured discussion format for groups of 4-20 people.

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Arrangement** | Inner circle (participants) + Outer circle (observers) |
| **Text** | A shared text, data set, problem, or artifact to examine |
| **Facilitator** | Asks questions only. Never states opinions. |
| **Duration** | 20-45 minutes for inner circle, then switch |

### Facilitator's Role (진행자의 역할)

1. **Open** with a provocative question about the text
2. **Follow up** with Socratic questions based on responses
3. **Redirect** when the discussion drifts: "How does that connect to ___?"
4. **Include** quiet participants: "What's your reaction to what [name] said?"
5. **Deepen**: Use Type 2-5 questions to push beyond surface answers
6. **Close** with a synthesis question: "What's changed in your thinking?"

### Ground Rules (기본 규칙)

1. Refer to the text/evidence — opinions must be grounded
2. No hand-raising — speak when there's a natural pause
3. Address each other, not the facilitator
4. Build on what others say — "I agree with X because..." or "I'd push back on X because..."
5. Silence is OK — thinking time is productive

### Sample Seminar Flow

```
Phase 1: Opening (5 min)
├── Facilitator poses opening question
├── 2-3 initial responses

Phase 2: Core Discussion (20-30 min)
├── Facilitator uses Types 1-5 to deepen
├── Participants build on each other
├── Facilitator introduces Type 4 (counter-perspectives) at midpoint

Phase 3: Closing (5-10 min)
├── Type 6 questions: "What's the real question here?"
├── Each participant: "One thing that shifted for me was..."
```

## Process: Applying the Socratic Method (적용 프로세스)

### Step 1: Identify the Claim or Belief (주장/신념 파악)

What is the person asserting? State it back to them clearly.

### Step 2: Start with Clarification (Type 1) (명확화로 시작)

Before challenging, make sure you understand. Ask what they mean. Get examples.

### Step 3: Probe Assumptions (Type 2) (가정 탐구)

What's beneath the surface? What are they taking for granted?

### Step 4: Examine Evidence (Type 3) (증거 검토)

Is this backed by data, experience, or just habit? How strong is the evidence?

### Step 5: Introduce Alternative Views (Type 4) (대안적 관점 제시)

What would a critic say? What does the opposing evidence show?

### Step 6: Trace Implications (Type 5) (함축 추적)

If their claim is true, what follows? Are they willing to accept those consequences?

### Step 7: Reflect on the Inquiry (Type 6) (탐구 성찰)

Have we been asking the right questions? What have we learned about our own thinking?

## When NOT to Use the Socratic Method (소크라테스식 문답법을 쓰지 말아야 할 때)

| Situation | Why Not | Alternative |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| **Emergency** (긴급 상황) | No time for discovery. Lives at stake. | Direct instruction |
| **Pure factual query** (단순 사실 질문) | "What's the capital of France?" doesn't need Socratic inquiry | Just answer |
| **Person is emotionally flooded** (감정적으로 압도된 상태) | Questions feel like interrogation when someone is distressed | Active listening, empathy first |
| **Extreme power imbalance** (극단적 권력 불균형) | Boss using Socratic method on a new hire feels like manipulation | Teach first, Socratic later |
| **When you're just being passive-aggressive** (수동적 공격) | "Don't you think you should have done X?" is not Socratic — it's manipulative | Be direct (use Radical Candor) |
| **They already know the answer** (이미 답을 알고 있는 경우) | Pretending to ask when the answer is obvious is patronizing | Affirm and move on |

### The Manipulation Test (조작 테스트)

Ask yourself: "Am I genuinely curious about their answer, or am I leading them to MY predetermined conclusion?"

- If genuinely curious → Socratic Method is appropriate
- If leading to your answer → You're using questions as a weapon. Stop. Be direct instead.

## Anti-patterns (하지 말아야 할 것들)

1. **The Gotcha Trap** (함정 질문) — Using questions to trap someone into admitting they're wrong. Socratic method seeks truth, not victory.

2. **Infinite Regress** (무한 퇴행) — Asking "why?" forever like a 3-year-old. Know when understanding is sufficient.

3. **Question Bombing** (질문 폭격) — Rapid-fire questions without letting the person think. Leave silence after each question. Count to 10.

4. **Pretend Ignorance** (가짜 무지) — Feigning you don't know the answer when you obviously do. Patronizing. If you know, share — then ask what they think.

5. **Using Questions to Lecture** (질문으로 강의하기) — "Don't you think that if we consider X and Y, then obviously Z?" That's a statement with a question mark.

6. **Socratic Method as Power Play** (권력 행사로서의 소크라테스) — A senior leader grilling a junior employee "Socratically" is intimidation, not inquiry.

7. **Ignoring Emotional State** (감정 상태 무시) — If someone is distressed, anxious, or defensive, questions will feel like an attack. Attend to the emotion first.

8. **Never Offering Your View** (자신의 견해를 절대 안 밝히기) — Pure Socratic questioning in every interaction is exhausting. Sometimes people need your direct input. Read the room.

## Historical Context (역사적 맥락)

### Socrates (소크라테스, 469-399 BC)

- Athenian philosopher who wrote nothing — known only through his students (primarily Plato)
- Spent his life in the Athenian agora (marketplace) questioning citizens about virtue, justice, and knowledge
- His method: start with a confident claim → ask questions that reveal internal contradictions → arrive at *aporia* (productive confusion) → build better understanding
- Executed by Athens for "corrupting the youth" — i.e., teaching them to question authority
- His defense (*Apology*): "The unexamined life is not worth living"

### Modern Applications

| Field | Application |
|-------|------------|
| **Law** | Cross-examination; law school case method (소크라테스식 법학교육) |
| **Medicine** | Clinical reasoning; differential diagnosis through questioning |
| **Therapy** | Cognitive Behavioral Therapy uses Socratic questioning to challenge cognitive distortions |
| **Education** | Inquiry-based learning; Socratic seminars (K-12 and university) |
| **Management** | Coaching; helping reports find their own solutions |
| **AI/Ethics** | Examining assumptions in algorithm design and training data |

## Advanced Technique: The Elenchus (논박)

The classic Socratic move:

1. The interlocutor asserts a thesis (P)
2. Socrates draws out additional premises the person also believes (Q, R)
3. Socrates shows that Q and R logically imply NOT-P
4. The person must either abandon P or abandon Q/R
5. This *aporia* (productive confusion) is the beginning of real understanding

**Example**:
- Claim: "A good leader always has the answers." (P)
- "Do you believe a good leader builds strong teams?" (Q — they agree)
- "Do strong teams require members who think independently?" (R — they agree)
- "If a leader always has the answers, would team members develop independent thinking, or defer to the leader?"
- Contradiction exposed. Now real thinking begins.
