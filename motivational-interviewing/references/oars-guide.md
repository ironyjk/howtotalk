# OARS Quick Reference Guide (OARS 빠른 참조 가이드)

## Overview

OARS = Open questions + Affirmations + Reflections + Summaries

The four micro-skills of Motivational Interviewing. Use them in combination, with a 2:1 ratio of reflections to questions.

---

## Open Questions (개방형 질문)

### Purpose
Invite elaboration, exploration, and self-reflection. Cannot be answered yes/no.

### Starter Phrases
| Korean | English |
|--------|---------|
| ~에 대해 어떻게 생각하세요? | "What do you think about...?" |
| 어떤 점이 걱정되시나요? | "What concerns you about...?" |
| ~하면 어떤 점이 달라질까요? | "What would be different if...?" |
| 가장 중요한 것은 무엇인가요? | "What matters most to you?" |
| 어떤 것을 시도해 보셨나요? | "What have you tried so far?" |

### By MI Stage

**Engaging**:
- "What brings you here today?"
- "What would you like to talk about?"
- "What's been going on?"

**Focusing**:
- "Of everything you've mentioned, what feels most pressing?"
- "Where would you like to start?"

**Evoking**:
- "What would you like to see change?"
- "If things stayed exactly the same, what would that be like in 5 years?"
- "What are the best reasons for making this change?"
- "What would be different if you succeeded?"

**Planning**:
- "What do you think would work for you?"
- "What's a reasonable first step?"
- "Who could support you in this?"

## Affirmations (긍정/인정)

### Purpose
Recognize the person's strengths, values, and efforts. Build confidence and self-efficacy.

### Structure
Affirmations focus on **the person**, not your opinion of them.

### Templates

| Situation | Affirmation |
|-----------|-------------|
| They showed up | "It takes courage to be here and talk about this." |
| They're struggling | "Despite everything, you haven't given up. That takes real persistence." |
| They tried and failed | "You learned something important from that attempt." |
| They're ambivalent | "The fact that you're thinking about this carefully shows how seriously you take it." |
| They took a small step | "You made a choice to do something different. That's significant." |
| They have self-awareness | "You have a clear understanding of what's at stake." |

### Common Mistakes

- **Cheerleading** ("Great job!" "I'm so proud!") — This is about YOUR approval, not their qualities
- **Conditional** ("If you keep this up...") — Affirmations are unconditional
- **Disingenuous** (affirming something you don't actually see) — Must be authentic

## Reflections (반영)

### Purpose
The single most important MI skill. Shows you're listening AND moves the conversation deeper.

### Types and Examples

#### Simple Reflection (단순 반영)
Repeat or slightly rephrase what they said.

> Client: "I've been drinking more since I lost my job."
> Reflection: "Your drinking has increased since you became unemployed."

#### Complex Reflection (복합 반영)
Add unstated meaning, emotion, or implication.

> Client: "I've been drinking more since I lost my job."
> Reflection: "Losing your job left a gap, and drinking has been filling some of that space."

#### Amplified Reflection (증폭 반영)
Slightly overstate to invite the person to correct you toward change.

> Client: "I don't think my drinking is that bad."
> Reflection: "So your drinking hasn't caused any problems at all."
> Client: "Well, I wouldn't say NO problems..."

#### Double-Sided Reflection (양면 반영)
Reflect both sides of the ambivalence. Put Sustain Talk first, Change Talk last.

> Client: "I know I should cut back, but it's how I relax."
> Reflection: "On one hand, drinking is your main way to unwind, AND on the other hand, you're aware that cutting back would be better for you."

(Use "and" not "but" — "but" erases what came before it)

#### Feeling Reflection (감정 반영)
Name the emotion beneath the words.

> Client: "My boss keeps giving me more work and I can't say no."
> Reflection: "That sounds frustrating — like you're trapped between wanting to please and feeling overwhelmed."

### Reflection Stems

- "It sounds like..."
- "You're feeling..."
- "What I'm hearing is..."
- "So for you, it's about..."
- "Part of you wants... and part of you..."

## Summaries (요약)

### Purpose
Collect and organize what the person has shared. Shows deep listening. Creates transition points.

### Types

#### Collecting Summary (수집 요약)
Gather several things they've said.

> "Let me see if I have this right. You've mentioned that your health has been declining, that your partner has been worried, and that you don't feel as sharp at work as you used to. Did I miss anything?"

#### Linking Summary (연결 요약)
Connect something from earlier to something just said.

> "Earlier you mentioned that being a good parent is your top priority. And just now you said your kids have noticed changes in your behavior. How do those connect for you?"

#### Transitional Summary (전환 요약)
Signal a shift — from one topic to another, or from exploring to planning.

> "So to pull this together: you came in feeling uncertain, and as we've talked, you've identified several reasons why change matters to you — your health, your family, and your self-respect. You've also acknowledged some real challenges. Where would you like to go from here?"

### Summary Tips
- Include Change Talk prominently
- Include Sustain Talk briefly (shows you heard it)
- End with an open question
- Use their words, not yours

## OARS in Practice: Session Flow

```
Opening (2 min)
├── Open question: "What brings you in today?"
├── Reflection x2
├── Affirmation: "It took initiative to make this appointment."

Exploring (10-15 min)
├── Open question → Reflection → Reflection → Open question
├── Collecting summary every 3-4 exchanges
├── Affirmations when you notice strengths
├── Readiness ruler if appropriate

Deepening (10 min)
├── Double-sided reflections for ambivalence
├── Linking summaries
├── Evocative open questions

Closing (5 min)
├── Transitional summary
├── Planning open questions (if ready)
├── Final affirmation
```
