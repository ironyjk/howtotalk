# Motivational Interviewing — Theory & Background

## Origin & Creators

Motivational Interviewing (MI) was developed by William R. Miller (clinical psychologist) in 1983, later refined with Stephen Rollnick. Originally created for addiction treatment, it has since expanded to healthcare, education, criminal justice, and management. The definitive text is *Motivational Interviewing: Helping People Change* (3rd ed., 2013).

## The Spirit of MI (MI의 정신)

MI is NOT a technique. It's a **way of being** with people. The spirit has four elements:

### 1. Partnership (협력)
- MI is done **with** people, not **to** them (함께 하는 것이지, ~에게 하는 것이 아니다)
- You are not the expert on their life — they are
- Two experts in the room: you (on the topic) and them (on themselves)

### 2. Acceptance (수용)
Four components:
- **Absolute worth** (절대적 가치): Every person has inherent worth
- **Accurate empathy** (정확한 공감): Deep understanding of their experience
- **Autonomy support** (자율성 지지): Their right to choose, even poorly
- **Affirmation** (긍정): Recognize their strengths and efforts

### 3. Compassion (연민)
- Actively promote their welfare and prioritize their needs
- Not your agenda — their well-being
- Different from sympathy (feeling sorry) — compassion is active

### 4. Evocation (환기)
- Draw out what's already there — don't install motivation
- They have the seeds of change; you water them
- "What matters to YOU about this?" not "Here's why you should change"

## The Righting Reflex (교정 반사)

### What It Is
The Righting Reflex is the natural, well-intentioned urge to **fix** people — to tell them what's wrong and how to fix it. It's the #1 enemy of MI.

### Why It Backfires
When you argue FOR change, the other person reflexively argues AGAINST it. This is psychological reactance (심리적 반발). You literally push them further from change.

```
You: "You really should exercise more."
Them: "Yeah but I don't have time."
You: "Even 15 minutes would help."
Them: "You don't understand how busy I am."

-> You've made them rehearse their reasons NOT to change.
```

### The MI Alternative
```
You: "What would be different if you were more active?"
Them: "I'd probably feel better. Sleep better too."
You: "So energy and sleep matter to you."
Them: "Yeah, I've been exhausted lately."
You: "What might be a small step that could work with your schedule?"

-> THEY are now arguing FOR change.
```

## Change Talk vs Sustain Talk (변화 대화 vs 유지 대화)

### Change Talk — DARN-CAT (변화 대화)

Preparatory:
- **D**esire (욕구): "I want to..." / "I wish I could..."
- **A**bility (능력): "I could..." / "I might be able to..."
- **R**easons (이유): "I'd be healthier if..." / "My family would..."
- **N**eed (필요): "I have to..." / "Something needs to change..."

Mobilizing:
- **C**ommitment (다짐): "I will..." / "I'm going to..."
- **A**ctivation (활성화): "I'm ready to..." / "I'm willing to..."
- **T**aking steps (실행): "I already started..." / "Yesterday I..."

**Your job**: Evoke, recognize, and reinforce Change Talk. When you hear it, REFLECT it back.

### Sustain Talk (유지 대화)
Arguments for NOT changing: "I can't...", "It's not that bad...", "I've tried before..."

**Your job**: Don't argue against it. Roll with it. Reflect it and explore the ambivalence.

## The Readiness Ruler (준비도 척도)

> "On a scale of 1 to 10, where 1 is not at all ready and 10 is totally ready, how ready are you to make this change?"

Then the magic follow-up:

> "Why did you pick [their number] and not a **lower** number?"

This forces them to articulate reasons FOR change — their own Change Talk.

**Never ask why they didn't pick a HIGHER number** — that evokes Sustain Talk.
